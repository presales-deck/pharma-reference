01
Universal Framework
The phases every drug goes through

Regardless of whether it's a tablet, injectable, or biologic, pharmaceutical manufacturing always follows the same broad phases. Steps inside each phase differ by product — but the structure is universal and mandated by GMP regulations globally.

Analogy

Think of drug manufacturing like a high-stakes commercial kitchen with government inspectors watching at any time. You have a recipe (the formula), approved ingredients from vetted suppliers, a strictly controlled kitchen environment, mandatory quality checks before anything leaves, and complete records of everything — because if a patient is harmed, regulators need to trace exactly what was made, how, and when.

01
Raw Material Sourcing & Testing

Every ingredient — the active drug and all excipients (inactive ingredients: fillers, binders, coatings) — must come from approved suppliers, be tested on arrival, and be released by QC before entering manufacturing. Nothing goes in unless verified. Supplier qualification is a separate GMP requirement.

Entry Gate
02
Weighing & Dispensing

Precise quantities of each ingredient are weighed and dispensed in controlled environments. Errors here propagate through the entire batch. Most facilities require two-person verification — one person weighs, one independently checks. The Batch Manufacturing Record (BMR) is issued here and accompanies the batch throughout its entire life.

Critical Control Point
03
Manufacturing / Processing

The actual conversion of ingredients into a drug product. This is where processes diverge most dramatically by dosage form — granulation and compression for tablets, aseptic filling for injectables, fermentation and purification for biologics. See Section 02 for each type in detail.

Product-Specific
04
In-Process & Final QC Testing

Quality is checked at multiple points during manufacturing (in-process controls) and comprehensively at the end (finished product release testing). A batch cannot be distributed until every specified test passes. Tests cover physical properties, chemical potency, and microbiology.

Release Gate
05
Packaging & Labelling

Finished product is packaged and labelled under controlled conditions. Labelling errors — applying the wrong label — are among the most common GMP violations and trigger recalls. Line clearance (complete cleaning and verification between products) is mandatory before any packaging run starts.

High Error Risk
06
QA Release & Distribution

Batch records are reviewed by Quality Assurance. A Qualified Person (QP in EU) or equivalent formally certifies the batch before distribution. The product enters the supply chain under controlled temperature and humidity. BMRs are retained for years after the product's expiry date.

What is a Batch Manufacturing Record (BMR)?

Every batch generates a BMR — a complete document recording every step taken, every measurement made, every deviation observed, and every person involved. Think of it as the birth certificate and life record for a batch of medicine. If a patient is harmed months later, regulators trace it through the BMR. Under 21 CFR Part 211, BMRs must be retained for at least one year after the batch's expiry date.

02
By Product Type
Manufacturing by dosage form

The manufacturing process depends entirely on the final product form. A tablet and an injectable require completely different equipment, environments, and regulatory controls. Click each tab to explore how that dosage form is made, step by step, with a real-world example.

Tablets and capsules are the most common dosage forms — roughly 70–80% of all medicines. The manufacturing process combines the active drug with excipients (inactive helpers: binders, fillers, disintegrants, lubricants) and compresses or fills them. Complexity ranges from a 4-ingredient immediate-release tablet to a 20-ingredient modified-release system.

Two main manufacturing routes
Wet granulation: mix powders, add liquid binder to form granules, dry them, then compress. Better for poorly compressible APIs. More steps, more controls.

Direct compression: blend powders and compress directly. Fewer steps, lower cost — but only works when the API and excipients have good flow and compressibility.
Route selection has regulatory implications — changes post-approval require ICH Q12 assessment and may need regulatory notification
What excipients actually do
Binders — hold the tablet together (PVP, HPMC)
Fillers/diluents — bulk the tablet to handleable size (lactose, MCC)
Disintegrants — help the tablet break apart in the stomach (croscarmellose)
Lubricants — prevent sticking to punches during compression (magnesium stearate)
Coatings — protect, mask taste, or control release (HPMC film, Eudragit)
Each excipient must be qualified, sourced from approved suppliers, and tested on receipt — same as the API

1
Dispensing & sieving
API and excipients are weighed, dispensed, and sieved to break up lumps and ensure uniform particle size. Done in a dispensing booth with local exhaust ventilation to protect operators from potent dust exposure. Every weighed item is logged in the BMR with quantity, operator ID, and time.
Environment
Humidity-controlled area. Potent APIs require fully enclosed systems or respiratory PPE
2
Dry mixing (blending)
API and intragranular excipients are mixed in a high-shear mixer or tumble blender. Blend uniformity is critical — a poorly blended batch means some tablets contain too much drug, others too little. Sampling must prove uniformity across the entire blend volume before proceeding.
In-process test
Blend uniformity sampling — multiple locations, multiple time points per validated protocol
3
Wet granulation
A liquid binder solution is added to the powder blend while mixing at high shear. The wet mass forms granules — small dense aggregates that compress better than raw powder. Granule size and density must hit defined specifications. Over-granulating makes tablets too hard; under-granulating means poor binding.
Key parameters
Impeller speed, chopper speed, liquid addition rate, granulation end-point (power consumption)
4
Drying — fluid bed dryer
Wet granules are dried in a fluid bed dryer — hot air flows upward through the granule bed, evaporating moisture. Loss on Drying (LOD) is the critical in-process test, typically targeting 1–3%. Too wet causes caking and microbial risk; too dry causes friability and poor compression behaviour.
Critical test
Loss on Drying (LOD) — moisture measured at defined intervals during drying. Final LOD must meet spec before milling
5
Milling & sizing
Dried granules pass through a mill screen to achieve uniform particle size distribution. Oversized agglomerates are broken down; excessive fines are minimised. This controls the flow properties going into the tablet press — poor flow produces weight variation, which means dose variation.
In-process test
Particle size distribution by sieve analysis or laser diffraction. D90 typically ≤500 µm
6
Lubrication & final blending
Extragranular excipients — lubricant, glidant, and disintegrant — are added and blended briefly. This step is critical and unforgiving: too little lubricant and tablets stick to punch faces; too much magnesium stearate creates a hydrophobic barrier causing dissolution failure. Blend time is typically validated to 2–5 minutes only.
Critical risk
Over-lubrication with Mg stearate → hydrophobic film → dissolution failure → drug not released properly in patient
7
Tablet compression
Blend feeds into a rotary tablet press — up to 500,000 tablets/hour on modern equipment. Upper and lower punches compress the blend into tablets. In-process testing every 15–30 minutes: weight, hardness, thickness, friability, and disintegration. Tablet weight directly determines dose — it is the most critical in-process control in tablet manufacturing.
In-process controls
Weight (USP ±5%), hardness, thickness, friability (<1%), disintegration — checked every 15–30 min
8
Film coating (if required)
Tablets tumble in a coating pan while a polymer solution is spray-applied. Coating purposes: taste masking, moisture/light protection, or modified drug release (delayed or sustained). For modified-release products, coating thickness and uniformity directly control how much drug is released at each time point — it becomes a safety-critical specification tied to the dissolution test.
Post-coating tests
Weight gain %, appearance, dissolution profile (critical for MR coatings — defines release rate)
9
Final release testing & QA certification
Full finished-product testing before release: assay (how much drug?), dissolution (does it release correctly?), content uniformity (same dose in every tablet?), microbial limits, appearance, identification. All results reviewed by QA. Batch released only if every specification is met without exception — a single failing result requires investigation and potentially batch rejection.
Release tests
Assay, dissolution, content uniformity, microbial limits, description, identification, related substances
Real-world example
Metformin 500mg tablet — type 2 diabetes, one of the world's most manufactured tablets

Metformin HCl is the API — highly water-soluble but poorly compressible, making wet granulation typical. The challenge: metformin is hygroscopic (absorbs moisture from air), so manufacturing must occur in low-humidity environments. The immediate-release version disintegrates in ~15 minutes; the extended-release version uses a polymer matrix (HPMC) to deliver drug over 8–12 hours — requiring a tighter dissolution specification and different coating system. Both versions appear on the WHO Essential Medicines List.

Key process challenges
  • High drug load (~80% API) — little room for excipients
  • Hygroscopic API — humidity must be below 45% RH throughout
  • High tablet weight (500–1000 mg) — requires robust compression force
  • ER version: dissolution profile is the defining release specification
Key release tests (IR)
  • Assay: 95.0–105.0% of label claim
  • Dissolution: ≥80% released at 45 minutes (USP Apparatus 2)
  • Content uniformity: RSD <6% across 10 units
  • Microbial limits: TAMC ≤1000 CFU/g

Injectables go directly into the body — bloodstream, muscle, or tissue — bypassing every natural defence. This is why they are the most tightly regulated dosage form. There is zero tolerance for microbial contamination. A single contaminated vial reaching a patient can cause sepsis or death within hours. Every design decision in injectable manufacturing exists to prevent, detect, and eliminate contamination.

Two sterilisation strategies
Terminal sterilisation: product is filled into its final container and then sterilised — typically by autoclaving (121°C steam). Preferred by regulators because it sterilises the final closed unit. Only possible if the drug is heat-stable.

Aseptic processing: the product is sterilised by filtration before filling, then filled into pre-sterilised containers under such clean conditions that contamination cannot occur. Required for heat-labile drugs — most biologics, some APIs. Higher risk, more complex to validate.
Sterile Product — governed by 21 CFR Part 211 Subpart D (US) and EU GMP Annex 1 (2022 revision)
Cleanroom grades explained
Grade A (ISO 5): the filling zone — HEPA-filtered laminar flow directly over open product. The most controlled environment in any industry.
Grade B: the immediate background environment surrounding Grade A.
Grade C: preparation areas — solution preparation, component washing.
Grade D: gowning rooms, support areas.

Each grade has strict limits for airborne particles (>0.5 µm and >5 µm) and viable organisms (CFU/m³) that must be monitored continuously and trended.
EU GMP Annex 1 (2022 revision) is the current global benchmark for sterile manufacturing facility design and control

1
Water for Injection (WFI) preparation
WFI is the purest pharmaceutical water grade — produced by distillation or reverse osmosis/electrodeionisation. It must meet stringent specifications. Endotoxins — fragments from bacterial cell walls — cause fever even without live bacteria. A drug can be sterile yet still cause a pyrogenic (fever) reaction if endotoxin is present. WFI is circulated continuously to prevent biofilm formation in the distribution system.
WFI Spec (Ph.Eur/USP)
Conductivity ≤1.3 µS/cm, TOC ≤500 ppb, endotoxin ≤0.25 EU/mL, sterile
2
Bulk solution compounding
API dissolves in WFI with excipients — buffers (to control pH), tonicity agents (to match body fluid osmolarity), stabilisers, and preservatives if a multi-dose vial. pH is adjusted precisely. The solution is made up to final volume and checked for clarity. For biologics, temperature, pH, and oxygen exposure must be tightly controlled — the protein can degrade or aggregate under stress.
In-process tests
Assay, pH, osmolality, appearance (clear, colourless, essentially particle-free)
3
Bioburden reduction & sterile filtration
The bulk solution passes through a 0.22 µm sterilising-grade membrane filter. At 0.22 µm, no bacterium or fungus can pass — this removes all biological contamination before filling. Filter integrity testing (bubble point or diffusion test) is mandatory before AND after filtration. A failed post-use filter integrity test means the filtration cannot be confirmed effective — the batch must be rejected.
Critical test
Filter integrity pre- and post-filtration. Post-use failure = mandatory batch rejection and investigation
4
Container preparation — washing & depyrogenation
Glass vials are washed (to remove particulates) then passed through a depyrogenation tunnel — heated to 250°C+ for a defined time period. At these temperatures, endotoxins are destroyed. Sterilisation at 121°C is not sufficient to destroy endotoxins — a separate, more extreme heat step is required. Rubber stoppers are washed, sterilised, and siliconised to enable smooth automated machine handling.
Depyrogenation
250°C / 30 min minimum. Tunnel temperature profile validated; endotoxin challenge study per ISO 13408
5
Aseptic filling — Grade A environment
Sterile solution fills into sterile vials using filling needles under Grade A HEPA-filtered laminar airflow. This is the highest-risk step — the brief moment when product is exposed to the environment. Modern facilities use RABS (Restricted Access Barrier Systems) or isolators to physically separate operators from the filling zone. Fill volume is verified continuously by weight feedback. Every operator action in or near Grade A is documented in real time.
In-process controls
Fill volume every 15 min, environmental monitoring (viable + non-viable particles), gowning verification, interventions logged
6
Stoppering, crimping & 100% visual inspection
Vials are immediately stoppered and crimped as they exit the filling zone. Then every single vial undergoes 100% visual inspection — either by trained human inspectors or automated inspection machines. Inspectors and machines check for particulate matter (visible particles in the solution), container defects (cracks, chips), fill volume anomalies, and cosmetic issues. A vial with visible particles must be rejected — no exceptions under any GMP framework.
Inspection standard
100% inspection per USP <790> / Ph.Eur. 2.9.20. Typical reject rate 0.1–2% on a well-controlled batch
7
Sterility testing & 14-day hold
Samples are incubated for 14 days in two growth media (for aerobic and anaerobic organisms) to confirm no viable contamination. This test samples only a tiny fraction of the batch — real sterility assurance comes from process control, not this test. Modern facilities increasingly use parametric release — certifying sterility from validated process data rather than waiting 14 days for a test that rarely finds anything if the process was controlled correctly.
Hold time
14-day sterility test incubation before batch can be released. Any failure triggers full investigation
Real-world example
mRNA COVID-19 vaccine (BNT162b2 / Comirnaty) — a new class of injectable manufacturing

The Pfizer-BioNTech vaccine introduced manufacturing challenges the industry had never faced at scale. mRNA is exquisitely fragile and cannot enter cells on its own — it must be encapsulated in lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) for delivery. The drug substance (mRNA) and the delivery system (LNPs) are manufactured separately then combined. Ultra-cold storage (-70°C during distribution) adds GDP complexity not seen in conventional injectables. The entire process is aseptic — mRNA cannot survive any terminal sterilisation step.

Unique manufacturing challenges
  • mRNA degrades rapidly — ultra-cold chain (-70°C) throughout
  • LNP particle size must be 70–120 nm — nanoscale precision required
  • Encapsulation efficiency must be >85%
  • New analytical methods required — no compendial standards existed
Key release tests
  • mRNA integrity (capillary electrophoresis)
  • Encapsulation efficiency (Ribogreen assay)
  • Particle size and polydispersity index (DLS)
  • Sterility, endotoxin <2 EU/dose, appearance

Topical products — creams, ointments, gels, lotions, and transdermal patches — are applied to the skin. They don't need to be sterile (skin is not a sterile surface), but they must be microbiologically controlled, physically stable over shelf life, and cosmetically elegant. The core manufacturing challenge is creating the right texture, consistency, and drug distribution — then proving that formulation holds for 2–3 years.

Cream vs ointment vs gel
Creams: oil-in-water or water-in-oil emulsions. Most common topical form. Light, spreadable, cosmetically elegant. Thermodynamically unstable — can phase-separate over time if formulation is poor.

Ointments: semisolid, anhydrous oil-based (petrolatum, lanolin). No water — more stable than creams, better skin-barrier protection, but greasier. Simpler to make.

Gels: polymer network swollen with solvent. Aqueous (carbomer gel) or hydroalcoholic. Very uniform drug distribution when API is dissolved. Common for anti-inflammatories and antifungals.
Dosage form selection affects drug penetration depth, patient compliance, and product stability — all captured in the formulation development report
Transdermal patches — controlled delivery through skin
Patches deliver drug through skin into the bloodstream over hours or days (nicotine, fentanyl, estradiol, contraceptive patches). Manufacturing is pharmaceutical coating on a moving web — a drug-in-adhesive or drug-in-reservoir layer is coated onto a backing film at precise thickness, then covered with a release liner.

Uniformity of the drug layer across millions of patches is the critical quality attribute — variation in coating thickness means variation in dose delivered to the patient.
Modified Release — the drug delivery rate profile IS the product specification; it must be defined and validated

1
Oil phase preparation
All oil-soluble ingredients — waxes, oils, oil-soluble emulsifiers, and oil-soluble API (if any) — are combined in a jacketed manufacturing vessel and heated to 65–80°C. Heating melts solid fats and waxes and ensures complete dissolution of all components. Temperature uniformity across the vessel is critical — localised overheating can degrade heat-sensitive excipients or the API.
Critical parameter
Temperature uniformity ±2°C across vessel. Validated heating profile required per batch size
2
Water phase preparation
All water-soluble ingredients — purified water, humectants, water-soluble emulsifiers, preservatives (parabens, phenoxyethanol), and water-soluble API — are combined and heated to the same temperature as the oil phase. pH of the water phase is adjusted before mixing, as pH affects API stability, preservative efficacy, and emulsion stability.
In-process test
pH of water phase before emulsification — spec defined per formulation development data
3
Emulsification
The water phase is added to the oil phase (or vice versa, depending on emulsion type) under high-shear mixing. This breaks the immiscible phases into tiny droplets (1–10 µm) stabilised by emulsifiers. Droplet size directly determines product texture, appearance, stability, and drug penetration into skin. This step defines the character of the entire product — it must be exactly reproducible batch to batch.
Critical parameters
Mixer speed, temperature at mixing, addition rate of second phase — all validated per batch size
4
Cooling with continuous mixing
The emulsion cools slowly under continuous gentle agitation. Rapid or uneven cooling causes wax crystallisation that produces a grainy, unacceptable texture. Heat-sensitive ingredients — fragrance, certain preservatives, thermolabile APIs — are added during the cooling phase at defined temperatures (typically 35–40°C) to avoid degradation while ensuring complete incorporation.
Addition temperature
Heat-sensitive materials added at 35–40°C. Continuous temperature monitoring throughout cooling
5
Final homogenisation & in-process QC
Final pass through a homogeniser ensures uniform droplet size throughout the full batch. In-process checks: viscosity (must be within spec for filling — too thick won't fill; too thin behaves like a lotion), pH, appearance, and assay. The batch cannot proceed to filling until all in-process specifications are confirmed met.
In-process tests
Viscosity (Brookfield, defined spindle/speed), pH, appearance, assay before proceeding
6
Tube/jar filling & sealing
Cream fills into tubes or jars at controlled temperature to maintain correct viscosity for consistent fill weight. Tube filling machines crimp and seal the base of the tube immediately after filling. Fill weight is checked every 15–30 minutes. Tube seal integrity is verified — a leaking tube is a quality defect and a contamination risk. Batch reconciliation: all filled units accounted for vs materials dispensed.
In-process controls
Fill weight per unit, seal integrity testing, visual appearance of filled units, batch reconciliation at end
Real-world example
Hydrocortisone 1% cream — one of the world's most widely manufactured topical corticosteroids

Hydrocortisone is dissolved in the oil phase. The stability challenge: corticosteroids degrade in the presence of water and metal ions — so the formulation uses pH control (acidic, 4.5–5.5), chelating agents (EDTA inactivates metal catalysts), and antioxidants. The product must remain physically stable (no phase separation) and chemically stable (no degradation beyond limits) for a 2–3 year shelf life — proven through ICH Q1A stability studies at controlled temperature and humidity conditions.

ICH Q1A stability programme
  • Long-term: 25°C / 60% RH for 24+ months (real shelf-life claim)
  • Accelerated: 40°C / 75% RH for 6 months (early prediction)
  • Intermediate: 30°C / 65% RH if accelerated shows change
  • Photostability: ICH Q1B light exposure study
Key release tests
  • Assay: 90.0–110.0% of label claim
  • Related substances — degradation products below defined limits
  • Microbial limits: TAMC ≤10² CFU/g, TYMC ≤10¹ CFU/g
  • Viscosity, pH (4.5–5.5), appearance, preservative content

Biologics are medicines made from or by living organisms — bacteria, yeast, or mammalian cells. Unlike small-molecule drugs, biologics are large, complex molecules that cannot be fully defined by chemical formula alone. The process IS the product — even small changes in manufacturing conditions can alter the molecule itself, changing its safety and efficacy. This makes biologic manufacturing the most complex, most capital-intensive, and most regulated category in all of pharma.

Why biologics are fundamentally different
Aspirin is the same molecule regardless of who makes it. A monoclonal antibody is a protein with a complex 3D structure that depends on how it folded during cell culture, what post-translational modifications occurred, and how it was processed downstream.

Two factories making "the same" antibody with slightly different cell culture conditions can produce different glycosylation profiles (sugar chains on the protein), which can alter immune response, half-life, and efficacy in patients. This is why biosimilar approval requires extensive analytical and clinical comparability studies.
Biologic — "The process is the product." Any manufacturing change requires a formal comparability exercise
Main types of biologics
Monoclonal antibodies (mAbs): largest category — made in mammalian cells (CHO). Examples: adalimumab (Humira), trastuzumab (Herceptin), pembrolizumab (Keytruda)

Recombinant proteins: insulin, erythropoietin, clotting factors — expressed in bacteria, yeast, or mammalian cells

Vaccines: attenuated virus, inactivated virus, protein subunits, or mRNA-LNP platforms

Cell & gene therapies: most complex — the drug is living cells or viral vectors delivering genetic material
Each type requires its own distinct manufacturing platform, equipment, and analytical methods

1
Cell bank thaw & seed train expansion
Manufacturing starts from a frozen Master Cell Bank (MCB) — vials of genetically engineered cells stored in liquid nitrogen. A Working Cell Bank (WCB) vial is thawed and cells expanded progressively through increasingly larger vessels — from a flask (mL scale) to 50L, 200L, eventually 2,000–20,000L production bioreactors. This seed train takes 2–4 weeks. The cell bank is characterised extensively: identity, genetic stability, and freedom from viruses, bacteria, and mycoplasma.
Critical asset
Cell bank in dual-location LN₂ tanks. Loss or contamination = months of manufacturing downtime
2
Production bioreactor — cell culture
The production bioreactor runs fed-batch for 10–21 days. Temperature (37°C), pH (7.0–7.4), dissolved oxygen, CO₂, agitation, and feeding schedule are tightly controlled. The cells secrete the antibody into the culture broth. Daily monitoring: cell density, viability, glucose, lactate, dissolved O₂, and product titre. Deviations here directly alter the molecular quality of the final product.
Daily monitoring
Viable cell density, viability %, glucose, lactate, pH, dissolved O₂, daily product titre (HPLC or Protein A assay)
3
Harvest & clarification
At end of culture, bioreactor contents are harvested. Dead cells and debris are removed by centrifugation followed by depth filtration while retaining the antibody. The clarified harvest — the liquid containing antibody, culture media, and cellular proteins — proceeds to purification. Harvest must be processed quickly to prevent protease enzymes from degrading the product.
Transition point
Upstream → Downstream. Typical antibody titre at harvest: 2–15 g/L depending on cell line and process
4
Protein A capture chromatography
The defining purification step for monoclonal antibodies. Protein A resin binds antibodies with extraordinary specificity — almost nothing else sticks. The harvest flows through the column; the antibody is captured; everything else washes through. The antibody is eluted by lowering pH. A single Protein A step achieves ~99% purity from an enormously complex mixture — this is what makes mAb manufacturing economically viable.
Typical outcome
>99% purity; dramatic reduction in host cell proteins (HCPs), DNA, and process-related impurities
5
Viral inactivation & polishing chromatography
Because mammalian cells can harbour latent viruses, two orthogonal viral clearance steps are mandatory: (1) Low pH treatment (pH 3.5 / 60 minutes) — inactivates enveloped viruses. (2) Virus-retentive nanofiltration (20 nm pore) — physically removes small non-enveloped viruses. Further polishing chromatography steps (ion exchange, hydrophobic interaction) remove remaining host cell proteins, DNA, and aggregates (misfolded antibody clusters that can trigger immune reactions in patients).
Regulatory requirement
ICH Q5A — viral clearance studies must demonstrate ≥4 log₁₀ reduction for each independent step
6
Formulation, UF/DF & aseptic fill-finish
Purified antibody undergoes ultrafiltration/diafiltration (UF/DF) — concentrated to target concentration and buffer-exchanged into the formulation buffer. Stabilisers (sugars like trehalose, surfactants like polysorbate 80) are added to prevent aggregation during storage and shipping. Then sterile filtered (0.22 µm) and filled aseptically into vials or pre-filled syringes. Mechanical stress during pumping must be minimised — shear forces cause protein aggregation.
Sensitive step
Pumping shear → protein aggregation. Filling parameters (speed, pressure, nozzle design) validated to minimise mechanical stress
Real-world example
Adalimumab (Humira) — world's best-selling biologic, anti-TNF monoclonal antibody for autoimmune diseases

Made in Chinese Hamster Ovary (CHO) cells. End-to-end manufacturing takes approximately 60 days from cell bank thaw to final product release. The molecule's glycosylation profile — the pattern of sugars attached to the antibody — affects its half-life, Fc-effector function, and immunogenicity. This profile is directly controlled by cell culture conditions and is one of the key attributes checked in biosimilar comparability studies. Humira biosimilar approval required multiple analytical, PK, and clinical studies because "same amino acid sequence" is not sufficient to demonstrate "same medicine."

Critical quality attributes
  • Glycosylation profile (G0F, G1F, G2F glycoforms) — process-controlled
  • Aggregation (<2% high molecular weight species by SEC-HPLC)
  • Charge variants (acidic/basic isoforms by IEX-HPLC)
  • Biological activity (TNF-α binding potency by cell-based assay)
Key release tests
  • Potency by cell-based bioassay (TNF-α neutralisation)
  • Protein concentration (UV A280)
  • Purity by SEC-HPLC, IEX-HPLC
  • Sterility, endotoxin, appearance, pH, osmolality

Oral liquids — solutions, suspensions, emulsions, and syrups — are drugs in liquid form taken by mouth. Essential for patients who can't swallow tablets: paediatric patients, elderly patients, those with dysphagia. Solutions are simplest (drug dissolved, every spoonful identical). Suspensions are more complex — the drug is insoluble and must stay evenly distributed. Settling is the enemy of dose uniformity in a suspension.

Solutions vs suspensions — a critical difference
Solutions: API fully dissolved — every spoonful contains exactly the same dose. Simple to manufacture but requires the API to be soluble at the required concentration. Examples: paracetamol solution, cough syrups, oral electrolytes.

Suspensions: API insoluble, present as fine particles dispersed throughout. Critical challenge: if particles settle, a patient pouring from the bottom of a poorly-mixed bottle gets 5× the intended dose; from the top, almost nothing. "Shake well before use" is a patient safety requirement, not just good practice.
Suspensions require extensive rheology development — viscosity and particle size control the settling rate throughout shelf life
Preservatives — essential but tightly regulated
Oral liquids are aqueous systems — water supports microbial growth. Any multi-dose bottle opened repeatedly needs a preservative to prevent it from becoming a bacterial culture. Common preservatives: sodium benzoate, potassium sorbate, methylparaben.

Regulatory challenge: preservative concentrations are tightly limited (especially in paediatric products — parabens raise safety concerns in neonates). Minimum effective concentration must be demonstrated by Antimicrobial Effectiveness Testing (AET) per Ph.Eur. 5.1.3 / USP <51>.
Paediatric products — subject to additional excipient safety scrutiny per ICH E11(R1) and EMA paediatric guidelines
1
Purified Water preparation
Purified Water (not WFI — oral products don't require the highest water grade) is the primary solvent. The water system must be monitored continuously for microbial count and conductivity. Water is typically circulated continuously in a heated loop to prevent biofilm formation in pipes — stagnant pharmaceutical water becomes contaminated rapidly and can invalidate entire manufacturing campaigns.
Water spec (Ph.Eur.)
Conductivity ≤4.3 µS/cm at 20°C, TAMC ≤100 CFU/mL, no specified organisms
2
Compounding — dissolving or dispersing
For solutions: excipients (sweeteners, viscosity modifiers, buffers, preservatives) dissolve first, then API is added and dissolved. pH is adjusted to target range — pH affects API stability, taste, and preservative efficacy. For suspensions: API is micronised (particle size reduced to 1–10 µm) before dispersion, then wetted with surfactant and suspended in the viscous vehicle. Particle size of the suspended API is a critical quality attribute that must be controlled.
In-process tests
pH, assay, appearance, particle size (suspensions by laser diffraction), viscosity before filling
3
Bulk filtration (solutions) & in-process QC
Solutions are filtered through a 0.22 µm or coarser filter (not for sterility — for clarity and particle removal only). Suspensions cannot be filtered without losing the drug particles. Bulk in-process checks: assay confirms correct drug concentration, pH within spec, viscosity within spec for filling, appearance. Any out-of-spec result stops the batch for investigation before filling commences.
Suspension check
Particle size distribution (laser diffraction). D90 typically <50 µm for acceptable mouthfeel and dose accuracy
4
Filling into bottles & sealing
Liquid fills into glass or plastic bottles (HDPE or PET). For suspensions, the bulk must be kept under continuous mixing during filling to prevent settling in the holding tank — the dose in the first bottle must be identical to the last. Child-resistant caps (legally required in most markets) are applied and torque-tested for proper sealing. Fill volume is checked every 15 minutes throughout the run.
In-process controls
Fill volume every 15 min, cap torque testing, container integrity, dose uniformity (suspensions sampled top/middle/bottom)
Real-world example
Amoxicillin 250mg/5mL oral suspension — antibiotic for paediatric use globally

Amoxicillin is unstable in aqueous solution — it hydrolyses (breaks down) rapidly in water. The manufacturing solution: the product is supplied as a dry powder blend and reconstituted by the pharmacist or patient just before use by adding a defined volume of water. Manufacturing at the factory produces the dry powder blend only; the suspension is formed at the point of dispensing. This means the factory step is dry blending and powder filling — simpler than liquid manufacturing, but requiring tight control of particle size and blend uniformity to ensure correct dose after reconstitution.

Dry powder blend controls
  • Blend uniformity — amoxicillin must be uniformly distributed throughout the powder
  • Particle size — affects reconstitution rate and final suspension quality
  • Moisture content — moisture triggers rapid hydrolysis degradation
  • Reconstitution time — must fully suspend within 60 seconds of mixing with water
Post-reconstitution shelf-life tests
  • Assay ≥90% of label claim at end of reconstituted shelf life (typically 14 days at 2–8°C)
  • Particle size distribution — no crystal growth during storage
  • pH within spec throughout storage
  • Microbial limits — preservative efficacy must hold for 14 days

Inhaled products deliver drug directly to the lungs — for local effect (asthma, COPD) or systemic absorption via the lung surface. The defining challenge: the drug particle must be 1–5 µm to reach the deep lung, must be delivered in an exactly reproducible dose, and must deposit in the right part of the lung. Particle size is everything — too large and particles deposit in the throat; too small and they're exhaled. This makes inhaled manufacturing among the most technically demanding in pharma.

Three main delivery systems
pMDI (pressurised Metered-Dose Inhaler): drug dissolved or suspended in liquefied propellant (HFA). Each actuation delivers a metered dose. Manufacturing involves pressure filling with liquefied propellant. Most widely used globally.

DPI (Dry Powder Inhaler): micronised powder inhaled by the patient's own breath. No propellant. More sensitive to humidity — moisture affects particle aggregation and lung deposition. Device engineering and powder formulation are inseparable.

Nebuliser solutions: drug dissolved in aqueous solution, aerosolised by a bedside device. Manufactured like an oral liquid but often sterile.
Device and drug product are co-developed and co-validated — they cannot be separated at regulatory submission
APSD — the defining quality test
For inhaled products, the critical quality attribute is not just "how much drug" but "where does it deposit in the lung." Aerodynamic Particle Size Distribution (APSD) — measured by cascade impaction — characterises the fraction of each dose reaching different parts of the respiratory tract based on aerodynamic diameter.

Fine Particle Fraction (FPF): proportion of particles <5 µm — these reach the lower airways where therapeutic effect occurs.
MMAD: mass median aerodynamic diameter — the particle size where half the dose is larger and half smaller.
Inhaled — APSD by cascade impaction (NGI or ACI) is the primary release test per Ph.Eur. 2.9.18 / USP <601>

1
API micronisation — jet milling
The inhaled API must be reduced to a mass median aerodynamic diameter (MMAD) of 1–5 µm. Achieved by jet milling — particles collide with each other at high velocity in a stream of compressed gas, fracturing to the required size. The resulting micronised powder is extremely fine, cohesive, and prone to electrostatic charging. Particle size distribution is validated and checked on every API batch before it can be used in manufacturing.
Target PSD
MMAD 1–5 µm. D90 typically <10 µm. Measured by laser diffraction after every micronisation batch
2
Carrier lactose preparation & qualification
For DPI, micronised API blends with coarse lactose carrier particles (63–150 µm). Drug adheres to the lactose surface. On inhalation, drug detaches from the carrier and reaches the lung; the larger lactose deposits in the throat harmlessly. Lactose grade (particle size, surface area, surface energy) directly affects drug adhesion and therefore FPF. This is why inhaled manufacturers often qualify single lactose suppliers and treat the carrier as a critical excipient.
Critical attribute
Lactose surface area and particle size must meet tight specifications. Change in lactose grade = full APSD re-evaluation required
3
Drug-carrier blending
Micronised API is blended with carrier lactose under controlled conditions. The blending energy must be validated precisely — over-blending reduces fine particle fraction (API embeds too deeply in lactose surface and won't detach during inhalation); under-blending produces non-uniform drug coating on carrier and poor content uniformity. Relative humidity during blending must be controlled as moisture alters surface adhesion forces.
In-process test
Content uniformity of blend — multiple location samples. Humidity during blending <30% RH throughout
4
Filling — under strictly controlled humidity
DPI powder fills into blister packs, capsules, or reservoir devices under strictly controlled relative humidity — typically below 30% RH (often 15–25%). Moisture causes micronised particles to aggregate — clumps cannot be re-dispersed to single particles on inhalation, dramatically worsening APSD and reducing lung deposition. Humidity control in the filling area is as critical as cleanroom classification is for sterile products.
Environmental control
RH <30% during all powder handling and filling operations. Temperature and humidity continuously monitored and recorded
5
APSD testing & Delivered Dose Uniformity (DDU)
The primary quality release tests. Delivered Dose Uniformity (DDU) tests from across the full device life (beginning, middle, and end) — each dose must fall within ±25% of label claim per Ph.Eur. 2.9.28. APSD by Next Generation Impactor (NGI): the aerosol fires through impaction stages separating particles by aerodynamic size — drug on each stage is assayed. FPF and MMAD must meet product-specific specifications.
Release tests
DDU across device life, APSD (FPF, MMAD by NGI), assay, moisture content (Karl Fischer), microbial limits
Real-world example
Fluticasone/Salmeterol DPI (Advair Diskus / Seretide) — asthma combination inhaler with two APIs

A combination of two micronised APIs (fluticasone propionate for inflammation; salmeterol for bronchodilation) blended with lactose. The challenge: both APIs have different optimal particle sizes and different adhesion energies to lactose — the blend must deliver both at their correct doses simultaneously with the correct APSD for each. Any change to the lactose grade changes the adhesion characteristics and therefore the APSD of both APIs — requiring complete analytical revalidation. This is why inhaled product manufacturers often single-source lactose and treat carrier specification as a regulatory commitment.

Manufacturing sensitivities
  • Lactose surface energy affects both APIs differently — single supplier mandatory
  • Blending energy validated to ±20 RPM — over/under blending changes FPF
  • Humidity <20% RH throughout all powder handling operations
  • Device and formulation are co-validated — not interchangeable between manufacturers
Key release specs
  • Fluticasone DDU: label claim ±25% across full device life
  • Salmeterol DDU: label claim ±25% across full device life
  • FPF defined minimum % independently for each API
  • Moisture content <0.5% by Karl Fischer titration

Quality Control
How quality is tested and verified

Quality Control operates at every stage — incoming materials, during manufacture, and at the finished product. The GMP principle: you cannot test quality into a product. Quality must be built in through controlled processes. Testing is verification of what was already built in — not a substitute for process control.

📥
Incoming Material Testing
Every raw material — API and all excipients — is tested on arrival before use. A Certificate of Analysis (CoA) from the supplier is not sufficient alone; the manufacturer must independently verify it. Testing confirms the material is what the supplier says it is, at the purity stated.
  • Identity (IR spectroscopy, HPLC)
  • Purity / related substances
  • Water content (Karl Fischer)
  • Microbial limits (for applicable materials)
  • Elemental impurities (ICH Q3D)
⚗️
In-Process Controls (IPC)
Testing performed during manufacturing at defined critical control points. IPC allows deviations to be caught and corrected during the process — rather than discovering a failed batch only at the end. IPC specifications are typically wider than release specs; they flag trends before problems become failures.
  • Tablet weight, hardness, thickness every 15–30 min
  • Blend uniformity (multiple-location sampling)
  • Fill volume / fill weight during filling
  • pH and assay during compounding
  • Environmental monitoring in clean areas
🔬
Finished Product Release Testing
Comprehensive testing of the finished batch before distribution. Every specification must pass — a single failing test means the batch is rejected unless investigation establishes an assignable non-product cause. Release testing is the final gate before patients receive the medicine.
  • Assay (potency / drug content)
  • Related substances / degradation products
  • Dissolution / release profile
  • Content uniformity (all solid dosage forms)
  • Microbial limits / sterility (sterile products)
  • Endotoxin / pyrogen (injectables)
📊
Stability Testing
Every product must be proven to remain within specification for its entire stated shelf life. Stability studies run at defined temperature and humidity conditions per ICH Q1A. Data from these studies establish the expiry date on the label and prove the stated storage conditions (e.g. "store below 25°C") are sufficient.
  • Long-term: 25°C/60% RH (real-time shelf-life claim)
  • Accelerated: 40°C/75% RH (early prediction)
  • Intermediate: 30°C/65% RH (when accelerated shows change)
  • Photostability: ICH Q1B light exposure
  • In-use stability: after opening (multi-dose products)
🌡️
Environmental Monitoring (EM)
For products made in classified cleanrooms, the environment itself is a product quality attribute. EM programmes continuously measure airborne particles and viable microorganisms. Trends must be actively monitored — a gradual increase in environmental counts predicts a contamination event before it occurs.
  • Viable air sampling (active impaction and settle plates)
  • Surface contact plates (equipment, walls, gown surfaces)
  • Non-viable particle counts (continuous, ISO 14644)
  • Personnel monitoring (glove prints, gown surface)
  • Water system monitoring (PW, WFI loops)
🔎
Out-of-Specification (OOS) Investigations
When any test result falls outside specification, a formal OOS investigation is mandatory per FDA 2006 OOS guidance. The two-phase process: Phase 1 (lab investigation — was it a testing error?) and Phase 2 (full manufacturing investigation — was the batch actually defective?). True product OOS results mean batch rejection.
  • Phase 1: analyst error check, instrument verification, retest per protocol
  • Phase 2: manufacturing record review, retained sample testing
  • Root cause analysis and CAPA required regardless of outcome
  • Regulatory notification if distributed batch is affected

GMP in Practice
What GMP actually means on the factory floor

Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) is not a certification or a piece of paper — it is a set of behaviours, systems, and controls that must be continuously maintained. A regulator walking your facility is not checking boxes. They are assessing whether your organisation has a functioning quality culture. Here is what that looks like in practice.

What GMP actually requires

GMP regulations require that manufacturers adequately control manufacturing operations. This includes establishing strong quality management systems, obtaining appropriate quality raw materials, establishing robust operating procedures, detecting and investigating product quality deviations, and maintaining reliable testing laboratories. If a manufacturer's processes are not controlled, the product's quality is not assured — regardless of what the release testing shows. — FDA 21 CFR Part 211 framework

SOPs
Standard Operating Procedures

Every manufacturing step, every cleaning activity, and every test method is governed by a written SOP. SOPs are not suggestions — they are the controlled, approved way to perform a task. Deviating from an SOP without authorisation is itself a GMP violation, regardless of outcome. SOPs must be periodically reviewed, kept current, and training must be formally documented for every person who follows them.

Foundation Document
CAPA
Corrective & Preventive Action

When something goes wrong — a failed batch, a deviation, an OOS result, a complaint — CAPA is the formal process for fixing it and preventing recurrence. Corrective action addresses the specific event. Preventive action addresses the root cause so it doesn't happen again elsewhere. Regulators look for CAPA effectiveness — did the fix actually work? Ineffective CAPAs are a major inspection finding.

Continuous Improvement
Change Control
Managing Every Change

In GMP, you cannot change anything — a supplier, an equipment parameter, a room layout, a SOP, a test method — without formal change control. The change must be assessed for impact on product quality, regulatory filings, and validated states. Some changes require regulatory notification or approval before implementation. Change control records are among the first documents an inspector requests.

Risk Management
Validation
Proving Your Process Works

Before a process is used to make product for patients, it must be validated — demonstrated to consistently produce product meeting all specifications. Process validation is a three-stage lifecycle per FDA 2011 guidance: process design (understanding), process qualification (demonstrating it works at production scale), and continued process verification (monitoring that it continues to work over time using real production data).

ICH Q10 Aligned
Data Integrity
The Foundation of Trust

All data generated during manufacturing must be ALCOA+: Attributable (who generated it?), Legible (can it be read?), Contemporaneous (recorded at time of action?), Original (not a copy?), Accurate — plus Complete, Consistent, Enduring, and Available. Data integrity failures — deleting results, backdating records, recording only passing results — are among the most serious GMP violations and can result in criminal prosecution.

Critical Compliance Area
PQR
Annual Product Quality Review

Every year, for every product, a formal PQR compiles all batch data, OOS results, deviations, stability data, complaints, and CAPA status into a single review document. The PQR assesses whether the process is in a state of control — is quality consistent year over year? Are there trends? Are specifications still appropriate? Regulators review PQRs during inspections as a window into overall manufacturing performance.

Annual Requirement
Where AI fits into GMP manufacturing

AI systems in GMP manufacturing must operate within — not outside — this GMP infrastructure. An AI model that flags deviations is generating data subject to data integrity requirements. An AI that recommends process adjustments is effecting a change that must go through change control if it falls outside validated parameters. An AI-generated SOP is a document that requires qualified human review and approval before use. FDA's 2025 AI credibility framework does not replace GMP — it sits on top of it. The architectural question is whether the AI system generates GMP-compliant evidence as a byproduct of normal operation, or whether it requires a separate evidence-construction exercise every time a regulator asks.
