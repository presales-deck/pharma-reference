Chapter 01 — What is ERP & Where Does Manufacturing Start?
The Starting Point
Every Tablet Starts With
an Order in ERP

Before a single gram of powder is weighed, before any equipment starts — a customer order lands in an ERP system. Understanding ERP is understanding where pharmaceutical manufacturing truly begins.

Definition
What is ERP?
ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) is the central business software that runs the entire company — sales, procurement, inventory, finance, HR, and production planning. In pharma, systems like SAP or Oracle ERP receive customer orders and translate them into manufacturing instructions that eventually reach the factory floor.

▶ SAP ERP Intro (Video)    ↗ SAP ERP Overview
🏢

One System for the Whole Company

ERP connects every department — Sales, Finance, Procurement, HR, Production — into a single unified platform. Everyone sees the same data in real time.

📦

Demand → Supply Chain

When a customer order arrives, ERP automatically calculates what needs to be manufactured, what materials need to be ordered, and when everything must be ready.

🏭

Creates Work Orders for MES

ERP creates a "Work Order" (WO) — essentially a production ticket — that flows down to MES. MES then guides the actual manufacturing step by step.

ERP Architecture
Core ERP Modules in Pharma

A pharma ERP isn't one thing — it's a suite of modules. Each module owns a piece of the business. Here's what each one does and how it connects to manufacturing.

📋
Sales & Distribution (SD)
Manages customer orders, pricing, delivery schedules. When a hospital orders 10,000 packs of Metformin, it starts here.
⚙️
Production Planning (PP)
Translates sales orders into production schedules. Creates Work Orders. Runs MRP (Material Requirements Planning) to figure out what needs to be made.
🛒
Materials Management (MM)
Manages procurement — raises purchase orders for raw materials (API, excipients), receives goods into inventory, manages stock levels.
🧪
Quality Management (QM)
Manages inspection lots, quality notifications, vendor certifications, and holds materials that have failed QC testing.
💰
Finance & Controlling (FI/CO)
Tracks cost of production, batch costing, accounts payable/receivable, and financial reporting. Captures yield losses and waste costs.
🏬
Warehouse Management (WM)
Manages storage locations, material movements, staging areas, and finished goods inventory ready for dispatch.
🔬
Recipe / BOM Management
Stores the Bill of Materials (BOM) and routing instructions — the master list of ingredients and process steps for each product.
👥
HR & Plant Maintenance (PM)
Manages equipment maintenance schedules, calibration records, and workforce planning for production shifts.
Roles & Responsibilities
Who Does What in ERP?

Different people in the organisation have specific roles in ERP. Understanding who touches ERP and what they do helps you see how a manufacturing batch gets initiated.

Role ERP Module What They Do in ERP Output to Manufacturing
Demand Planner Sales / PP Analyses sales forecast, creates demand plan for next quarter. Determines how many batches of each product to make. Planned Orders (auto-created by MRP)
Procurement / Buyer MM Reviews material stock, raises Purchase Orders for API and excipients, coordinates delivery timelines with suppliers. Raw material availability for production
Production Planner PP Converts Planned Orders into Work Orders, schedules them to specific manufacturing lines, assigns batch numbers. Work Orders sent to MES for execution
Warehouse / Stores WM / MM Receives incoming raw materials, confirms receipt in ERP, stages dispensed materials for production, receives finished goods. Material availability confirmed, goods movements posted
QA / QC QM Releases or rejects raw material lots in ERP after QC testing. Reviews batch records, releases finished batches for sale. Released material status; batch disposition
Production Manager PP / CO Monitors production progress against the schedule, reviews yield reports, manages exceptions and escalations. Confirmed batch completions, yield posted to ERP
Finance Controller FI / CO Tracks batch production costs vs standard, posts goods issue (raw materials consumed) and goods receipt (finished goods created). Financial settlement of production orders

Chapter 02 — The ERP → MES Handoff
The Critical Bridge
How ERP Talks to MES —
The Work Order Handoff

The moment a Work Order leaves ERP and arrives in MES is where business planning ends and physical manufacturing begins. This handoff is one of the most critical integrations in a pharma plant.

ERP (SAP / Oracle) — L4
What ERP sends to MES
Work Order number & batch number
Product code and description
Batch size (quantity to manufacture)
Recipe / BOM reference (what ingredients, what steps)
Scheduled start & end dates
Manufacturing line / equipment assignment
Released raw material lot numbers
Required delivery date
⇄
Integration
MES — L3
What MES sends back to ERP
Batch completion confirmation
Actual yield (finished tablets produced)
Actual materials consumed (goods issue)
Production start & end timestamps
Batch status (Released / On Hold / Rejected)
Deviation count & classification
Equipment utilisation data
Operator IDs and shift information
Why MES matters: MES sits at L3 and receives Work Orders from ERP. It translates those Work Orders into electronic batch records (eBR) that guide operators through every manufacturing step. When the batch is done, it sends completion data back to ERP so inventory, finance, and QA records are automatically updated.

▶ MES & ERP Integration Explained
Inside MES
What Happens When MES Receives a Work Order
📄

1. eBR is Generated

MES takes the Work Order and automatically creates an Electronic Batch Record — a step-by-step digital instruction set for operators, populated with the approved recipe parameters.

🔍

2. Materials are Verified

MES checks that all required raw material lots have been released by QA and are physically available in the dispensing area. Production cannot start until all materials pass this check.

🏭

3. Equipment is Checked

MES confirms each piece of required equipment has been cleaned (cleaning record checked), calibrated, and is available — not already running another batch.

👤

4. Operators are Assigned

Operators log into MES with their credentials. MES verifies they have the right training and qualifications to perform the required steps on this product.

▶️

5. Execution Begins

With all checks passed, MES unlocks the first step of the eBR. The batch clock starts. Every action from this point — weights, parameters, signatures, times — is captured automatically.

📤

6. Completion Reported Back

When the last step is signed off in MES, it automatically posts completion to ERP — updating inventory, triggering financial settlement, and flagging the batch for QA batch record review.

Chapter 03 — What is MES?
Manufacturing Execution System
MES: The Digital Brain
of the Factory Floor

If ERP is the brain of the business, MES is the brain of the factory. It takes business instructions from ERP and turns them into real-time shop floor control — guiding every operator, every machine, every step.

Simple definition: MES is the software layer that sits between the business (ERP) and the machines (SCADA/PLC). It tells operators exactly what to do, guides them through how to do it, captures proof they did it right, and records everything for GMP compliance and traceability.

▶ Intro to ERP Systems   ↗ MESA International
🎯

Execute

Drives step-by-step execution of batch instructions. Operators follow the eBR on screen — no paper, no guessing.

📊

Track & Trace

Every action — who, what, when, which lot, which equipment — is logged with a timestamp creating a complete audit trail.

🔒

Enforce Controls

Steps cannot be skipped. Out-of-spec results automatically raise deviations. Electronic signatures enforce accountability.

⚡

Real-Time Visibility

Supervisors see live batch status, IPC results, and equipment status on dashboards — enabling instant decisions.

🔗

Integrate

MES bridges ERP (L4) above with SCADA/PLC equipment (L1/L2) below — acting as the translation layer of the plant.

📋

Comply

Supports FDA 21 CFR Part 11, EU Annex 11, and GMP requirements. Replaces paper batch records with compliant eBRs.

Chapter 04 — What Are OSD Tablets? Anatomy & Components
OSD — Oral Solid Dosage
What's Inside a Tablet?
Understanding What We're Making

Before we walk through how tablets are manufactured, you need to understand what a tablet actually is. Every component has a critical function — and MES ensures every single one is correctly accounted for.

Tablet Composition

The Layers of a Tablet

🔵 Film Coating
Outer protective layer — controls appearance, protects API, can control release rate. e.g. Opadry II
🟢 Lubricants & Glidants
Prevents sticking to press punches (Mg Stearate) and improves powder flow (Colloidal Silica)
🟣 Disintegrants & Binders
Disintegrants help tablet break apart in gut (Croscarmellose). Binders hold granules together (PVP).
🟠 Diluents / Fillers
Add bulk to tablet — Microcrystalline Cellulose (MCC), Lactose. Makes tablet large enough to handle.
🔴 API (Active Core)
The drug molecule — Metformin HCl 500mg. This is what actually treats the patient.
Critical Quality Attributes

What Makes a Good Tablet? (CQAs)

💊 Tablet Weight
500 mg ± 5%
Ensures correct dose in every tablet
💪 Hardness
80–130 N
Too soft = breaks; too hard = won't dissolve
📏 Thickness
4.5–5.0 mm
Must fit blister pack tooling consistently
🕐 Disintegration
< 15 min
Tablet must break apart in stomach to release drug
💧 Dissolution
Q ≥ 80% @ 30 min
80% of API must dissolve within 30 min in test media
🔬 Content Uniformity
95–105% label
Every tablet must have the same amount of API
🧪 Friability
≤ 1.0%
Must not crumble/chip during packaging & transport
💧 Moisture (LOD)
1.5–3.0%
Affects tablet stability and shelf life

🔄 Blending (Bin Blender)
AUTOMATEDIPCCRITICAL
Milled granules are blended with external phase (disintegrant, lubricant) in a bin blender. Blend uniformity is critical — poor blending means inconsistent API content tablet to tablet.
BIN-004 — Bin Blender (300L IBC)
12 min at 12 RPM — granules + croscarmellose
Mg Stearate added at 12 min — blend for 3 more min only
Over-lubrication with Mg Stearate damages tablet dissolution — MES timer enforces this
RSD ≤ 5% (10 samples tested for API content by HPLC)
Rotation count, RPM, bin ID, all operator IDs logged
RSD > 5% → deviation raised, batch hold, re-blend evaluation needed

Chapter 06 — IPC Schedule & Full Batch Specifications
In-Process Controls
IPC Schedule: What Gets
Tested, When, and Why

In-Process Controls are the quality checks built into every stage of manufacturing. They catch problems early — before the entire batch is compromised. MES enforces every IPC; you cannot proceed without completing them.

Step IPC Test Specification Frequency What Fails Looks Like MES Action if OOS
Dispensing Weight verification API ±0.5%; Excip. ±1% Every material, every weigh Wrong dose in batch — critical patient risk Auto-deviation + weighing locked
Granulation Granule appearance, moisture LOD ≤5% post-granulation End of granulation Over-wet: poor drying; Under-mixed: poor uniformity Deviation if moisture OOS
Drying LOD (Loss on Drying) 1.5–3.0% Every 20 min + at end Too dry = brittle tablets; Too wet = poor compression, instability Batch hold + QA review
Milling Particle size (d50) 200–400 µm Post-milling Too coarse = poor compressibility; too fine = flow problems Deviation, re-mill evaluation
Blending Blend uniformity (BU) RSD ≤ 5% Post-blend (10 samples) Non-uniform blend = tablets with too much or too little API Batch hold + re-blend assessment
Compression Weight, hardness, thickness 500mg±5%, 80–130N, 4.5–5mm Every 30 min (20 tablets) Variable weight = variable dose; low hardness = friable tablets Auto-deviation, press adjustment
Compression Disintegration < 15 min Every 2 hrs (6 tablets) Tablet doesn't dissolve → drug not absorbed by patient Major deviation + QA review
Metal Detection Challenge test (Fe/SS/Non-Fe) Pass all standards Every 30 min Metal contamination in tablets → patient injury risk Production STOP
Coating Weight gain, appearance 3–4% weight gain Every 30 min Under-coat = poor protection; over-coat = slow dissolution Deviation, coating extension
Packaging Seal integrity, print check No empty pockets, clear print Every 30 min + start/end Empty blister shipped to patient; illegible label → dispensing error Line stop, investigate
Why MES is essential for IPC: In a paper-based system, operators manually record IPC results on paper forms — it's easy to skip, misrecord, or falsify. MES makes every IPC test mandatory — the batch record cannot advance to the next step until the IPC is entered and within specification. This is what GMP compliance looks like in practice.
