Overview
Types of GMP investigations

A GMP investigation is triggered whenever something departs from what was expected, approved, or specified. The type of investigation — OOS, OOT, deviation, contamination, complaint, or audit finding — determines the initial procedure, but all share the same underlying framework: detect → contain → investigate → root cause → CAPA → close.

🔍
OOS — Out-of-Specification
Trigger: any test result outside approved specification limits
The most formally regulated investigation type in pharma. Triggered when any analytical test result — assay, dissolution, microbial, identity, related substances — falls outside the limits defined in the product specification or compendial standard. Governed by specific FDA guidance with a mandatory two-phase investigation structure that cannot be short-circuited.
FDA OOS Guidance 2006 · 21 CFR 211.192 · 21 CFR 211.194
Mandatory 2-Phase Process
📈
OOT — Out-of-Trend
Trigger: result within spec but statistically anomalous vs historical data
An OOT result is still within specification — but it is statistically unusual relative to the established trend for that product and test. The concern is predictive: if the trend continues, the product may eventually fall out of specification before its expiry date. Most commonly identified in stability testing, where gradual degradation must be tracked against the expected rate. OOT is an early-warning system for approaching failures.
ICH Q10 · FDA Stability Guidance · 21 CFR 211.166
Trending-Based Detection
⚠️
Deviation / Non-Conformance
Trigger: any unplanned departure from an approved SOP or process parameter
The broadest category — any event where what was done differs from what was approved. Deviations range from minor documentation errors (wrong date format) to critical process failures (aseptic breach in a sterile fill area). Not all deviations require the same investigation depth — severity classification (Critical / Major / Minor) drives the investigation intensity and batch disposition decision.
21 CFR 211.192 · EU GMP Chapter 1 · ICH Q10
Severity-Classified
🦠
Contamination Investigation
Trigger: microbial, chemical, or particulate contamination found in product or environment
Triggered when contamination is found — in finished product, environmental monitoring samples, water system, or during manufacturing. For sterile products, any viable contamination in the aseptic core (Grade A or B) triggers a full investigation including manufacturing review, personnel gowning review, process simulation (media fill) review, and environmental mapping. The most urgent investigation type due to direct patient safety risk.
EU GMP Annex 1 (2022) · 21 CFR 211.113 · USP <71>
Patient Safety Critical
📢
Product Complaint Investigation
Trigger: complaint received from healthcare professional, patient, or distributor
Every product complaint must be evaluated to determine whether it indicates a manufacturing defect or safety issue. Complaint investigations assess: is the complaint credible? Does it indicate a distributed batch quality problem? Are other complaints of the same type trending upward? Could the issue represent an undeclared product defect requiring a recall or field safety action? Complaints are a real-world quality signal about batches already in patient use.
21 CFR 211.198 · EU GMP Chapter 8 · ICH Q10
Post-Market Signal
📋
Audit / Inspection Finding
Trigger: observation raised during internal audit, supplier audit, or regulatory inspection
Regulatory inspection observations (FDA Form 483 items, EU GMP non-compliance statements, MHRA observations) and internal audit findings both require formal investigation and CAPA. For regulatory findings, the response must be submitted to the regulatory authority within a defined timeframe (typically 15 business days for US, 30 days for EU). The quality of the investigation response often determines whether an observation escalates to a Warning Letter.
21 CFR 214.160 · 21 CFR Part 211 · EMA Compliance Procedures
Regulatory Response Required
The investigation hierarchy — which triggers which

Investigations are not always independent. An OOS result may trigger a deviation (the batch failed its specification). A deviation investigation may uncover a contamination source. A contamination investigation may lead to a complaint investigation if distributed product is affected. A complaint investigation may trigger a regulatory audit. Understanding how investigation types connect — and when one type must escalate into another — is as important as understanding each type in isolation.

02
OOS Investigation
The complete OOS workflow

The Out-of-Specification investigation is the most formally structured investigation in GMP. The FDA 2006 OOS Guidance mandates a specific two-phase process that cannot be skipped or reordered. Every step has documentation requirements. Every decision point — "is this a lab error?" — must be answered with evidence, not assumption.

The most important OOS rule

The original OOS result is always valid until invalidated by documented, specific, assignable laboratory cause. You cannot invalidate an OOS result simply because subsequent retests passed. You cannot average OOS results with passing results. You cannot release a batch while an OOS investigation is open without QA documented justification. A passing retest does not make a failing result go away — it raises an additional question: why did the two results disagree?

Phase 1A — Initial Laboratory Investigation

Phase 1A is performed immediately by the analyst who generated the result, reviewed by the QC supervisor. It must be completed before any retest is considered. The purpose is to determine whether there is a documented, specific reason the result cannot be considered valid — a laboratory error that can be definitively attributed. If found, the result is invalidated and a retest is performed with a new sample preparation. Phase 1A must never become a fishing expedition to find any possible justification to invalidate an inconvenient result.

1
Identify and document the OOS result
The analyst records the OOS result exactly as generated — no rounding to make it appear "borderline." The result, the test method, the specification limit, and the degree of deviation are all recorded contemporaneously. The QC supervisor is notified in the same work shift. A preliminary event notification goes to QA. The batch or lot associated with the sample is identified and placed on hold immediately.
Documentation
OOS report number assigned; batch hold documented; QA notification logged with time
2
Analyst self-review of laboratory work
The analyst carefully reviews their own work against the approved test method SOP. Check: Was the correct sample used — right lot, right sample point? Was the correct test method version followed? Were all reagents and reference standards in date and properly prepared? Were instrument settings correct? Was the calculation performed correctly? Were control samples within limits? Was sample preparation correct? This review is documented with each item checked and the result of the check.
Checklist items
Sample identity, method version, reagent status, standard preparation, instrument calibration status, calculation verification, control sample result
3
Supervisor review of raw data and audit trail
The QC supervisor independently reviews all raw data — instrument printouts, audit trail from electronic systems, chromatograms, spectral data, or dissolution profiles as applicable. Specifically looking for: anomalous peak shapes, baseline issues, integration errors, unexpected system suitability failures, or any indication the data may have been collected or processed incorrectly. Electronic audit trails must be reviewed — not just reported data.
Raw data review
Instrument audit trail, system suitability results, chromatogram visual inspection, integration review, all supporting calculations
4
Phase 1A conclusion — with evidence
One of three conclusions: (a) Assignable laboratory cause found — specific documented error identified, result may be invalidated with full documentation and a retest performed; (b) No assignable cause found — proceed to Phase 1B with a written retest protocol approved by QA before retesting begins; (c) Analyst confirms result is correct — proceed directly to Phase 2. The conclusion must be supported by specific evidence, not general statements. "We could not find anything wrong" is not an assignable cause.
Timeframe
Phase 1A completed within 1 business day of OOS detection. QA approves the Phase 1A conclusion before Phase 1B begins
Decision Point 1 — End of Phase 1A
Was a specific, documented, assignable laboratory cause identified?
YES → Invalidate result; document cause; retest with new preparation; if retest passes: investigation may close at Phase 1
NO → Open Phase 1B: QA-approved retest protocol required before any retesting
Phase 1B — Extended Laboratory Investigation

Phase 1B is triggered when Phase 1A finds no assignable laboratory cause. Before any retesting occurs, a written retest protocol must be reviewed and approved by QA. The protocol must pre-specify: how many retests, which analyst(s), from which retained sample, and what the acceptance criteria are for the retests. Ad hoc retesting — running additional tests until a passing result appears — is one of the most serious data integrity violations in pharmaceutical laboratory work.

Phase 2 — Manufacturing Investigation

Phase 2 is a full investigation of the manufacturing process that produced the sample. It begins with record review and expands to physical investigation as needed. The Phase 2 investigation must determine whether the OOS result reflects a true product quality problem. Every finding must be documented. The investigation cannot close with "no cause found" and release the batch — an inconclusive OOS mandates batch rejection.

1
BMR review — complete, line by line
The Batch Manufacturing Record for the affected batch is reviewed completely — not just the steps that seem relevant. Looking for: any unrecorded deviations, parameters that were at the edge of their specification, equipment that was not in its qualified state, materials with any non-conforming incoming test results, yield calculations that are slightly low or high, any step that was performed out of sequence. The BMR review must be documented — not just "reviewed and no issues found."
Documents reviewed
BMR all pages, deviation cross-references, yield calculations, equipment IDs, all operator signatures and dates
2
Equipment logbook and calibration review
Every piece of equipment used in manufacturing the affected batch is identified from the BMR. For each: review the equipment logbook for the relevant date — what was made before this batch? Was the equipment cleaned and cleaning verified? Check the calibration certificate — was the equipment within its calibration interval? Review any maintenance events. Particular attention to analytical instruments used for in-process testing — were they calibrated? Were system suitability results within criteria?
Key question
What was on this equipment before this batch? Was it cleaned? Who verified the cleaning and when?
3
Raw material review
Review the incoming test results (CoA and analytical report) for every raw material used in the affected batch. Were all materials within their specifications? Were there any borderline results on incoming testing? Check material lot numbers in the BMR against the incoming test records. Confirm that only released materials were used — check that the material status label was "Released" at the time of dispensing. Any material with even a marginal result on incoming testing becomes a hypothesis to investigate.
Documents reviewed
Incoming test reports for all materials in BMR, material status labels, supplier CoAs, temperature records during storage
4
Personnel and training review
Identify all operators who performed critical steps in the affected batch from the BMR signatures. Confirm that each operator has current, documented training on the relevant SOPs. Check training records — not just the training system — for completeness. Interview operators about the batch if the investigation timeline allows. Document interviews formally with date, participants, and content. If an operator recalls an unusual event that was not documented in the BMR, this itself is a finding requiring separate documentation.
Training records
SOP training records for each operator who signed the BMR. Verify training was before the batch date and on the current SOP version
5
Environmental and utility review
Review environmental monitoring data for the manufacturing area on the date of batch manufacture. Were there any environmental alert or action limit exceedances on or near the manufacturing date? Review temperature and humidity records for manufacturing areas and storage areas. Review water system data (purified water or WFI) for the relevant period. For sterile products, review particle count data and viable monitoring results. Any environmental anomaly — even below action limits — is a potential contributing factor.
Data reviewed
EM settle plates and air sample results, HVAC temperature/humidity records, water system TOC and conductivity, clean utility data
6
Reserve sample testing
Testing of the retained/reserve sample from the batch, ideally using an alternative validated method or a second laboratory. If the reserve sample also fails — the OOS is confirmed as a true product failure. If the reserve sample passes — there is a discrepancy between the original sample and the reserve that itself requires explanation (sampling error? degradation? testing issue?). Reserve sample results must be reported in full — including if they contradict the desired conclusion.
Critical rule
Both the failing and passing reserve sample results must be reported. Reporting only the passing result when the reserve sample was tested multiple times is data integrity violation
7
Scope assessment — other potentially affected batches
Based on the evidence gathered, identify all other batches that could have been affected by the same root cause. If the root cause is a contaminated raw material lot — assess all batches that used that material lot. If the root cause is an equipment malfunction — assess all batches processed on that equipment during the relevant period. If root cause is still unknown — assess all batches from the same campaign. Scope assessment is one of the most commonly cited Phase 2 deficiencies — it is typically done too narrowly.
FDA expectation
FDA explicitly requires that the scope be expanded beyond the triggering batch unless there is documented justification for limiting it
Decision Point 3 — Phase 2 Conclusion
What does the manufacturing investigation conclude about the OOS result?
TRUE PRODUCT FAILURE — Root cause identified in manufacturing; batch rejected; CAPA initiated; affected batches assessed
INCONCLUSIVE — No root cause found in either lab or manufacturing; batch must be rejected; cannot release on hope
LAB INVALIDATED — Phase 2 confirms Phase 1 lab error with manufacturing data support; batch potentially releasable with full QA justification
What you are prohibited from doing during an OOS investigation — FDA is explicit
✗
Average the OOS result with passing retests. Reporting an "average" of 88% (OOS) and 99% (retest) as 93.5% (pass) is fraud. Each result stands independently until invalidated with documented cause.
✗
Perform unlimited retests until you get a passing result. The number of retests must be pre-specified in the Phase 1B protocol. Running tests 4, 5, 6, 7... until you get a pass, then reporting only the passing results, is testing into compliance — a violation that has led to criminal prosecution.
✗
Release the batch while the investigation is still open. Unless QA has documented a specific, justified rationale, a batch in an open OOS investigation cannot be distributed. Distribution of product under active OOS investigation without documented QA justification is a critical violation.
✗
Close the investigation as "inconclusive" and release the batch. An inconclusive OOS investigation — where neither Phase 1 nor Phase 2 could identify the cause — mandates batch rejection. "We investigated but couldn't find anything" does not constitute a basis for releasing a batch that failed its specification.
✗
Discard the OOS result from the laboratory records. Every result generated during the investigation — including all failing results — must be retained in the full laboratory record. Selective reporting of only passing results is falsification regardless of the electronic system used.

Deviation Investigation
Planned, unplanned, and how to handle both

Deviation investigations are the most frequent investigation type in GMP manufacturing. Every unplanned departure from an approved process — no matter how small it seems in the moment — triggers a deviation investigation. The investigation must determine: what happened, why it happened, whether the batch is affected, and what must be changed to prevent recurrence. The severity of the deviation determines the depth of investigation required.

Minor deviations have no impact on product quality, patient safety, or process performance. They are typically administrative or documentation errors. The batch is usually not held, but the deviation must still be documented, root-caused, and assessed for trends — because a pattern of minor deviations can indicate a systemic quality system weakness.

The trap of treating minor deviations as unimportant

A single entry with the wrong date format is a minor deviation. Ten batches in the same area with wrong date formats is a training failure or SOP clarity issue. Twenty sites each with "minor" documentation deviations of different types is a systemic documentation culture problem. Regulators review APQR data and look specifically for trends in minor deviations that should have triggered systemic intervention but did not. Minor does not mean unimportant — it means limited immediate product impact. The process correction is still required.

A planned deviation — also called a temporary change or concession — is a pre-approved departure from a standard procedure for a specific, defined set of batches. It is not a failure of the quality system; it is the quality system working correctly to accommodate a known, bounded situation with proper oversight. Planned deviations must not become a substitute for proper change control for ongoing situations.

1
Identify and document the need for departure
The production or QA team identifies that a specific, time-limited situation will require departing from the standard approved procedure for a defined set of batches. The departure is described specifically: what step, what parameter, what alternative approach, for how many batches, for what time period. A vague planned deviation ("may need to use different supplier") is not acceptable — the specific alternative must be described.
Key requirement
Described before the batch starts. A retroactive "planned deviation" raised after the fact is a contradiction in terms and is treated as an unplanned deviation
2
Impact assessment before approval
QA assesses whether the proposed departure could affect product quality, safety, or efficacy. Assessment includes: scientific basis for believing the departure is acceptable, any additional testing required to confirm product quality, whether the departure affects validated state of the process, and whether regulatory notification is required. The assessment is documented and becomes part of the planned deviation record.
QA approval
Written QA approval is mandatory before the batch starts. No QA approval = the departure becomes an unplanned deviation upon detection
3
Change control trigger assessment
If the same planned deviation is needed for more than 2–3 batches or is expected to continue beyond its original scope, it must be converted to a permanent change through the formal change control process. Repeated planned deviations to the same procedure are a regulatory red flag — they indicate that the procedure no longer reflects actual practice and that the facility is using planned deviations to avoid the work of formal change control.
Conversion trigger
If the situation is expected to continue or recur: open a change control. A planned deviation is for genuinely exceptional, time-limited circumstances only
04
Root Cause Analysis
Methods, examples, and documentation

Root cause analysis is the structured process of identifying why an event occurred — not just what occurred. Every GMP investigation must document the RCA method used, the evidence considered, and the conclusion reached. "We discussed it and agreed it was operator error" is not an RCA. A documented, systematic method applied to the evidence is required. This section covers the four methods most commonly used in pharmaceutical investigations, with pharmaceutical-specific examples for each.

The most common RCA failure mode

"Operator error" as a root cause is almost never acceptable in a GMP investigation. It describes what happened — not why it was possible. If an operator made an error, the real root cause is almost always one of: the SOP was ambiguous; training was inadequate; the workspace design made the error easy to commit; there was no engineering control to prevent it; the workload or time pressure was unreasonable. Stopping at "operator error" means the CAPA will be "retrain the operator" — and the same error will occur again, committed by a different trained operator in the same poorly-designed system.

5-Why Analysis
Most Common
Asks "why?" iteratively — each answer becomes the next question — until the root systemic cause is reached. Deceptively simple but very effective at exposing the gap between symptom and cause. Requires discipline to keep pushing past the proximate cause. Works best for linear causal chains.
Best for: events with a clear causal sequence. Avoid when multiple independent causes are possible simultaneously.
Fishbone / Ishikawa Diagram
Organises potential causes into the 6M categories: Man, Machine, Method, Material, Measurement, Mother Nature (environment). Forces the team to systematically consider every cause category — not just the most obvious. Produces a visual diagram that becomes part of the investigation report. Prevents tunnel vision.
Best for: complex events with multiple possible causes. Effective for team-based investigations where brainstorming is valuable.
Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)
A top-down logic tree mapping all combinations of conditions that could produce the observed event. Uses AND/OR logic gates. AND gate: both conditions must exist simultaneously. OR gate: either condition is sufficient. Proves that an identified cause is necessary — not just possible. More rigorous than 5-Why for complex system failures.
Best for: sterility failures, complex multi-system failures, when you need to prove a cause is necessary not just plausible.
FMEA — Failure Mode & Effects Analysis
Systematically evaluates each process step for all possible failure modes, their effects, and the existing controls. In an investigation context: which failure mode in which process step most likely produced the observed outcome, given the existing control environment? Bridges process risk assessment and investigation finding.
Best for: confirming root cause probability, systematic review of all process steps, risk-ranked investigations where resources must be allocated.
5-Why worked example — tablet dissolution failure OOS investigation
Why 1
WHY?
The batch failed dissolution — only 65% released at 45 min (spec ≥80%). The tablets disintegrated slowly and released the drug below the required rate.
Why 2
WHY?
The tablet coating was thicker than the approved specification — weight gain was 5.8% instead of the approved 3.0–4.5% range. A thicker polymer coating slows drug release.
Why 3
WHY?
The coating pan operator continued spraying beyond the target weight gain because he was checking the weight against the wrong line in the BMR — the theoretical yield field rather than the coating weight gain specification field.
Why 4
WHY?
The BMR layout placed the coating weight gain specification and the theoretical yield fields adjacent to each other, formatted similarly, with no visual distinction between a critical process parameter and a yield calculation field.
Why 5 ✓
ROOT
Root cause: the BMR template was poorly designed. Critical in-process specifications were not visually differentiated from informational yield fields. The design made confusion possible — and in a multi-step process, likely. The corrective action is BMR template redesign, not operator retraining.
Documenting the RCA — what the record must show

The RCA documentation must show: which method was used and why it was appropriate; what evidence was considered; what hypotheses were generated and tested; which hypotheses were ruled out and why; what root cause was concluded; and how the root cause was verified (i.e. "if we fix this, would the event have been prevented?"). A root cause stated without the investigational logic — just the conclusion — is not adequate RCA documentation. The investigation record must show the reasoning, not just the answer.

Identify all batches potentially affected by the same root cause — same equipment, same material lot, same operator, same process run. Do not limit the scope to the triggering batch without documented justification.
✓
Assess patient safety risk — could any patient currently using distributed product be harmed? Is there an immediate health hazard? This question must be answered explicitly and documented — not left as an implication.
✓
Check distribution status — has the affected batch been distributed? To which markets? Can it be recalled? What is the status of the batch in the supply chain?
Distribution status determines regulatory notification obligations
✓
Assess regulatory notification requirement — does this event trigger a mandatory report to the regulatory authority? Time limits for notification vary by market and event type.
✓
Quarantine all potentially affected batches — until the investigation has cleared them or confirmed they are unaffected. Do not release batches from the affected scope without explicit QA justification.
✓
Document the impact assessment explicitly — including batches assessed and cleared. "Assessed other batches — none affected" is not documentation. List the batches, state what was reviewed, and state why they were cleared.
🔬
Document & Evidence Review
Investigation Phase
▼
✓
Review the BMR for the affected batch completely — all pages, all steps, all deviations cross-referenced. Document what was found and what was checked.
✓
Review equipment logbooks for every piece of equipment involved — cleaning records, previous product, calibration status at time of batch. Document the review with dates and findings.
✓
Review incoming test results for all raw materials used — not just the finished product results. Were any results borderline? Were all materials from approved suppliers?
✓
Review training records for all operators who signed the BMR — confirm training was complete before batch date, on current SOP versions.
✓
Review environmental monitoring data for the manufacturing area on the batch date — alert and action limit exceedances, trends, unusual results.
✓
Review any prior deviations or OOS events on the same process, same equipment, or same product — for prior pattern evidence. Prior events with the same characteristics strengthen a root cause hypothesis.
✓
Interview operators and supervisors involved — formally, with documented date, participants, and content. If an operator recalls an unrecorded event, document the interview and investigate the disclosure separately.
🧩
Root Cause Analysis & CAPA
Core Investigation
▼
✓
Select and apply a documented RCA method — 5-Why, Fishbone, FTA, FMEA, or combination. The method must be named in the investigation report. "Team discussed" is not an RCA method.
✓
Generate and test multiple hypotheses — do not commit to the first plausible cause. Test each hypothesis against the available evidence. Document which hypotheses were ruled out and why.
✓
Verify the root cause — ask: "If this cause did not exist, would the event have occurred?" If the answer is no, you have a necessary cause. If yes — you have a contributing factor but not the root cause.
✓
Do not accept "operator error" as root cause without at least two more levels of why: why was the operator able to make this error? What system permitted it?
✓
Define corrective action (CA) specifically — with named owner, specific due date, verifiable outcome. "Fix the issue" is not a CA. "Revise SOP-0041 Section 4.3 to specify coating weight gain acceptance criteria in bold" is a CA.
✓
Define preventive action (PA) that addresses the systemic cause — not just the specific instance. The PA prevents recurrence anywhere in the facility, not just for this product.
✓
Define effectiveness check with specific criteria — what data will prove the CAPA worked, collected by whom, over what time period. This must be defined before the CAPA is implemented, not added as an afterthought when closure is due.
✅
Closure Requirements
Final QA Sign-Off
▼
✓
Batch disposition is documented explicitly — "Released" or "Rejected" or "Destroyed" with the specific QA justification for the decision. Disposition cannot be implied — it must be a written, signed statement.
✓
All required sections of the investigation report are complete — event description, immediate actions, impact assessment, evidence reviewed, root cause analysis, CAPA, and disposition. No blank sections.
✓
All CAPA actions have named owners and due dates — before closure. CAPAs without these are incomplete regardless of whether the investigation summary is written.
✓
QA formal signature and date on closure — the QA reviewer must attest that the investigation is complete, the conclusion is supported by evidence, and all required actions are in place.
✓
Regulatory notification assessment is documented — whether notification was required and whether it was sent, or why it was not required. Not completing this assessment is itself a finding.
06
Scope & Impact Assessment
How far to extend every investigation

Scope assessment is one of the most consistently cited investigation deficiencies in FDA Warning Letters. The default human instinct is to investigate the minimum — the specific batch, the specific event. Regulators expect the opposite: start broad and narrow down with evidence. The investigation scope must encompass everything that could have been affected by the same root cause.

🔴
Critical — expand scope immediately
  • All batches made on the same equipment in the relevant period
  • All batches using the same raw material lot number
  • All batches manufactured by the same operator on critical steps (if training/competency issue)
  • All batches in the same stability station (if stability OOS)
  • All batches distributed in the same shipment (if cold chain excursion)
  • All products sharing the same manufacturing area (if environmental event)
🟠
Commonly missed scope items
  • Batches made before the detected event — the problem may have existed longer than detected
  • Other products sharing the same supplier material lot
  • Batches from prior campaigns using the same now-suspect equipment
  • Distributed lots — not just in-house product
  • Stability samples on stability — not just released product
  • Similar products with similar process steps (transfer of risk)
📋
How to document scope assessment
  • List every batch considered in scope assessment
  • State the criterion for inclusion (same equipment, same lot, same period)
  • For batches cleared: state the specific reason they were cleared
  • For batches held: document the hold and the assessment plan
  • Date the scope assessment — scope may expand as investigation develops
  • QA to confirm scope is adequate before investigation closes
✅
Narrowing scope — when it is justified
  • Root cause is specific to a unique event (e.g. power outage that affected only one equipment at one time)
  • Evidence from reserve samples and records proves other batches were unaffected
  • The failure mode is physically impossible to have occurred in other batches (e.g. equipment decommissioned before other batches were made)
  • All of the above must be documented — not implied
The scope assessment FDA Warning Letter pattern

The most frequent scope criticism in FDA Warning Letters reads: "Your firm failed to extend the investigation to include other batches potentially affected by the same root cause." The root cause identified was a supplier material quality issue — but the investigation covered only the single batch that failed, not the 12 other batches that used product from the same supplier lot. All 12 were already distributed. Narrow scope investigations protect no one — they protect the batch count, not the patient.

07
Failures & Pitfalls
What investigations get wrong

Investigation failures fall into predictable patterns. The same types of errors appear in FDA Warning Letters, consent decrees, and inspection 483 observations year after year. Understanding these patterns is as important as understanding the correct process — because the failures are systematic and preventable, not random.

The 10 investigation failure patterns — from FDA Warning Letters and inspection data
01
Predetermined conclusions. The investigation begins with a conclusion — "it was probably a testing anomaly" — and then gathers evidence to support it, ignoring contradictory evidence. A proper investigation generates hypotheses from the evidence, tests them, and reaches the conclusion last. Regulators are trained to recognise reverse-engineered investigation logic in the narrative structure of the report.
02
"Operator error" accepted as root cause without further investigation. In a GMP quality system, human error is a symptom — the root cause is the system that permitted the human error. SOP ambiguity, training gaps, workspace design, cognitive load, time pressure — any of these can cause an operator to make an error. If operator error is the conclusion, the CAPA must address why the system permitted the error, not just retrain the individual operator.
03
Scope deliberately limited to avoid inconvenient findings. An investigation that looks only at the batch that failed — without assessing whether the same root cause affected earlier batches — is doing the minimum required to justify releasing the current batch, not fulfilling the purpose of the investigation. FDA explicitly expects scope expansion unless there is documented evidence that restriction is justified.
04
CAPA with unverifiable or vague actions. "Retrain staff," "increase awareness," "remind operators," "improve communication" — these are not CAPAs. They are aspirations. A CAPA must be specific, attributable to a named person, due by a date, and verifiable by objective evidence. An unverifiable CAPA is an un-closed investigation gap that will be rediscovered at the next inspection.
05
Effectiveness check not completed. The CAPA was implemented — the SOP was revised, the training was delivered, the equipment was fixed. The investigation was closed. Six months later, the same event occurred again. If an effectiveness check had been designed and executed — verifying that the changed system actually prevented recurrence through data — the second event would have been caught earlier or the CAPA failure detected before recurrence. This is the most cited gap in global CAPA system reviews.
06
Timeline inconsistencies in the investigation narrative. The investigation report says the event was detected at 14:00, but the deviation was opened the following morning. The investigation report says an immediate containment action was taken at 14:30, but the BMR shows the equipment was used again at 16:00. Timeline contradictions either indicate sloppy documentation or retroactive record creation — both are serious findings. Timeline reconstruction should be one of the first tasks in any investigation.
07
Investigation closed before all CAPA actions are complete. The investigation narrative is finished and QA has signed the closure — but the CAPA item "validate the revised cleaning method" has a due date three months in the future and has not yet been performed. The investigation itself cannot be fully closed with open critical CAPAs — or the open CAPAs must be tracked as separate items with explicit acknowledgement that they remain open and the investigation was closed with them outstanding.
08
Recurring events with identical root causes. The same root cause appears in four separate investigation reports over 18 months — each investigated in isolation, each resulting in the same "retrain the operator" CAPA, each closed as complete. The fourth occurrence should have triggered a systemic review of the CAPA effectiveness for the first three events. Recurring events demonstrate that neither the investigation nor the CAPA system are working as intended.
09
Low deviation rates presented as evidence of quality. A facility with 4 deviations per year across 200 batches is not demonstrating excellence — it is demonstrating that deviations are not being captured. All manufacturing has process variability. The industry average for a well-managed facility is tens to hundreds of deviations per year depending on scale. Extremely low deviation rates are an inspection red flag, not a quality indicator.
10
Batch released on the basis of a "likely passing" retest without investigation completion. The OOS investigation is still open. Someone has decided informally that the batch "probably" passes based on one passing retest. The batch is released. The investigation is eventually closed with an inconclusive finding. This sequence — release before investigation conclusion — is a serious regulatory violation. A batch with an open OOS investigation requires documented QA justification to release, not an informal assessment.
Markers of a high-quality investigation — what regulators want to see
✓
The investigation logic is visible. The report shows which hypotheses were generated, how each was tested against the evidence, which were ruled out and why, and how the conclusion was reached. Reading the investigation tells the story of how the investigators thought — not just what they concluded.
✓
The scope was appropriately wide and then narrowed with evidence. The investigation began by identifying all potentially affected batches, materials, and equipment. The scope was then narrowed based on documented evidence — specific records reviewed, specific tests performed, specific observations made — that cleared items from the scope. The narrowing is justified, not assumed.
✓
The root cause is systemic, not personal. Even when an operator action was the proximate cause, the root cause identified is the system condition that permitted it — the ambiguous SOP, the absent engineering control, the inadequate training programme. The CAPA addresses the system, not just the individual.
✓
The CAPA has an effectiveness check with data. Six months after CAPA implementation, the report shows: deviation rate for this type of event is now zero where it was 3 per year; or the system suitability pass rate is now 99.5% where it was 94%; or the audit score for the area improved from 72% to 96%. Numbers, not assertions.
✓
Prior similar events were reviewed and referenced. The investigation explicitly searched for prior events with similar characteristics and referenced them. Where prior CAPAs exist, the investigation assessed whether the prior CAPA was effective — and if not, addressed why the prior intervention failed.
