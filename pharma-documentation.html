Plain Language GMP Documentation Reference — 2025

The paper trail that
keeps patients safe

GMP documentation is not bureaucracy for its own sake. Every record, every log entry, every investigation report exists for one reason: if a patient is harmed, or a batch is recalled, or a regulator walks in — you can prove exactly what was made, how it was made, who made it, and what was done when something went wrong. This is the documentation that makes that possible.

Document Landscape
The 12 core GMP documents

GMP manufacturing generates a specific set of documents — each with a defined purpose, required content, and regulatory basis. No document is optional. A missing or incomplete record is treated by regulators as equivalent to the activity not having occurred. "If it isn't documented, it didn't happen" is the most foundational GMP principle.

The cardinal GMP rule on documentation

If it isn't written down, it didn't happen. If it was written down incorrectly, it's a falsification. If it was written down but then corrected improperly, it's a data integrity violation. There is no such thing as an informal GMP record. Every entry in a GMP document — including a correction of a single digit — is a permanent, auditable part of the quality record and may be reviewed by a regulator years after the batch has left the facility.

02
Document Anatomy
What each document must contain

Each GMP document type has required content defined by regulation and industry guidance. Regulators know exactly what they expect to see in each document type. A missing section, an incomplete field, or an unsigned approval is a finding. Understanding what each document must contain — and why — is fundamental to both writing them and reviewing them during investigations.

The Batch Manufacturing Record is the most important document in pharmaceutical manufacturing. It is the executed, batch-specific record showing that a batch was produced according to the Master Batch Record. A BMR is opened for each batch when it is started and closed only after QA review and batch release. It travels with the batch from start to finish.

Master BMR vs Completed BMR — know the difference

Master Batch Record (MBR): The approved template — the recipe. Contains the product name, code, formula, equipment, step-by-step instructions, and blank fields for data entry. Controlled document — it cannot be changed without formal change control and re-approval. Completed Batch Record (BMR): The MBR with all data filled in for a specific batch. Contains actual batch number, actual materials used, actual measured values, operator signatures, and a record of any deviations. The MBR is the plan; the BMR is the proof.

Most common BMR deficiencies — what inspectors find
01
Retroactive entries without justification. Recording a step after it was performed without documenting why it wasn't recorded contemporaneously. The correction itself is not the violation; the absence of explanation is.
02
Corrections made incorrectly. A single line through the error with the correction, date, and initials is the only accepted method. White-out, scribbling out, overwriting, or using correction fluid is a data integrity violation — it destroys the original entry.
03
Blank fields without explanation. Every field in a BMR must be filled in. If a step was not performed (e.g. coating step for an uncoated tablet), the field must read "N/A — not applicable to this product" with initials. A blank field means the step was either skipped or not recorded — both are violations.
04
Deviations not cross-referenced. If a deviation occurred and a Deviation Report was raised, the BMR must explicitly reference the Deviation Report number. A standalone Deviation Report with no mention in the BMR is an incomplete quality trail.
05
Calculated yields not verified. Yield calculations must be independently verified — the person who calculated should not be the same person who checks. Yield outside the expected range that was not investigated is among the most serious BMR findings.

Standard Operating Procedures are the controlled instructions for performing every GMP task. They are not guidelines or suggestions — they are the approved method. Performing a GMP task in a way that deviates from the governing SOP — even if the deviation was an improvement — is a GMP violation. The SOP must be followed as written, or formally changed first through change control.

Common deficiency — Training gap
SOP was revised but some operators were trained only on the old version. Using the correct procedure but documented as trained on version 3 when version 5 is current = training deviation. Training must be completed before the new SOP is effective for that operator.
Common deficiency — Revision not triggered
The actual practice in the facility differs from what the SOP says — but no change control was initiated. Regulators view this as either the SOP being wrong, or the practice being wrong. Either way it is a violation. Common cause: informal "improvements" made on the floor without paperwork.
Inspection tip — SOP version control
Inspectors will ask operators to show them the SOP they're working from and check whether it matches the current approved version in the document management system. Unsupported paper copies of outdated SOPs found at workstations are a common and easily avoided finding.
Inspection tip — SOP vs actual practice
Inspectors observe operations and cross-check against the governing SOP in real time. If the operator performs step 4 before step 3 (even with good reason), that is a deviation. The SOP must reflect what is actually done — not an idealised version of what should be done.

Equipment and area logbooks are the chronological record of every event for a piece of equipment or classified area. They prove the equipment was clean, calibrated, and in a qualified state when used for a specific batch. During investigations — particularly contamination, cross-contamination, or OOS events — the logbook is the primary source of information about what happened in that area or on that equipment before the problem was detected.

Logbooks in investigations — why they matter

During a contamination or OOS investigation, the equipment logbook is typically the first document reviewed. It answers: What was made on this equipment before the affected batch? Was it cleaned in between? Who cleaned it? Was the clean verified? A logbook with a gap in cleaning records, or a product changeover without documented cleaning verification, immediately becomes the primary hypothesis for the investigation. Logbook entries that are backdated, imprecise, or missing are treated as potential evidence of undisclosed process failures.

A deviation is any unplanned departure from an approved SOP, specification, or process parameter. Deviations are not inherently bad — detecting, documenting, and managing them is a sign of a functioning quality system. What regulators penalise is not the presence of deviations, but the absence of them. A facility with no reported deviations is suspect: it implies either that problems are not being detected or that they are being concealed.

An Out-of-Specification (OOS) result is any analytical test result that falls outside the established acceptance criteria in a specification or approved procedure. OOS investigations are among the most scrutinised documents in any FDA inspection — they are subject to specific FDA guidance (2006 OOS Guidance) and must follow a defined two-phase process. Getting them wrong is one of the most common causes of Warning Letters.

OOS vs OOT — understanding the difference
OOS (Out-of-Specification)OOT (Out-of-Trend)
DefinitionResult falls outside the approved specification limit (e.g. assay result of 88% when specification is 90–110%)Result is within specification but statistically unusual relative to historical results for this product/batch (e.g. assay consistently at 103% for 20 batches; new result is 94% — still within spec but trending down)
Immediate actionMandatory formal OOS investigation per FDA 2006 guidance. Batch held; no release until investigation completeQA notification; informal investigation to understand the change; may not require formal OOS process unless investigation reveals specification risk
Who determines?QC analyst reports immediately to QC supervisor upon observing a result outside specification limitsTypically identified by QC through statistical process control (SPC) charts, trend analysis in stability data, or APQR review
Regulatory basisFDA OOS Guidance (2006), ICH Q10 Quality System. Specific two-phase investigation mandatedICH Q10 product quality monitoring. No specific two-phase mandate but must be documented and investigated
What you cannot do when you get an OOS result

You cannot: average the OOS result with retests to get an "average" that passes. You cannot perform unlimited retests until you get a passing result. You cannot discard the original OOS result without documented assignable cause. You cannot release the batch while the investigation is open. Every one of these actions has resulted in FDA Warning Letters and consent decrees. The 2006 FDA OOS Guidance is explicit: a passing retest result does not invalidate an original failing result — it must be explained.

1
Phase 1A — Initial laboratory assessment
The analyst reviews their work immediately upon observing an OOS result. Check: Was the correct sample used? Correct method? Correct reagents/standards in date? Correct calculation? Instrument functioning correctly? This check is performed by the analyst and reviewed by the QC supervisor. It must be documented in real time — not reconstructed later. If a clear, documented assignable cause is found (e.g. expired reagent used), Phase 1A is complete. The result is invalidated with documented cause and a retest is performed.
Timeframe
Immediately upon OOS detection. Supervisor notified same day. Documentation started immediately — not reconstructed later
2
Phase 1B — Additional laboratory investigation
If Phase 1A finds no clear assignable cause, Phase 1B expands the laboratory investigation. A new sample preparation from the same retained sample is tested, following a written, pre-approved retest protocol approved by QC supervisor or QA. Retesting must follow a defined protocol specifying how many retests, by how many analysts, with what acceptance criteria. Retesting cannot be open-ended. If additional retests all pass, with a documented explanation for the original failure, Phase 1 may close. If any retest fails, Phase 2 is triggered.
Critical requirement
Retest protocol must be pre-approved by QA before retesting begins. Ad hoc retesting without a protocol is itself a violation
3
Phase 2A — Manufacturing investigation
If Phase 1 cannot find a laboratory cause for the OOS result, Phase 2 begins — a full investigation of the manufacturing process that produced the sample. Review the BMR for the affected batch: were all steps followed? Any deviations? Correct materials used? Equipment clean and calibrated? Environmental conditions normal? Review operator training records. Review raw material CoAs. The investigation must assess whether the OOS result reflects a true product quality problem rather than a testing error.
Documents reviewed
BMR, equipment logbooks, raw material CoAs, operator training records, environmental monitoring data, stability data for the product
4
Phase 2B — Extended investigation & reserve samples
Testing of reserve/retained samples from the batch, possibly using an alternative validated method. If a different laboratory tests the retained sample and also gets an OOS result, the OOS is confirmed as a true product failure. If the reserve sample passes, a discrepancy investigation is required to explain the conflicting results. The Phase 2 investigation must include an assessment of all potentially affected batches — not just the batch in question. Other batches from the same campaign or using the same materials must be assessed.
Scope requirement
All batches within the scope of the potential root cause must be assessed — the investigation cannot be limited to only the triggering batch
5
Final assessment, disposition & CAPA
QA writes a final assessment: root cause conclusion, all evidence reviewed, batches assessed, disposition of the affected batch, CAPA initiated. If the OOS is confirmed as a true product failure, the batch is rejected. If invalidated by a documented laboratory assignable cause, the batch may potentially be released — with full justification. If root cause is inconclusive, the batch must be rejected. An inconclusive OOS investigation does not permit batch release.
Disposition rule
Inconclusive OOS = batch rejection. Root cause must be established to release. Cannot release on "passing average" or hope

CAPA — Corrective and Preventive Action — is the backbone of continuous improvement in a GMP quality system. Every deviation, OOS, complaint, audit finding, or trend should generate a CAPA if a root cause has been identified. A CAPA without a root cause is just an action item. A CAPA that addresses only the symptom — not the cause — will see the same problem recur, which is one of the most common inspection findings.

Corrective vs preventive — understanding the difference
Corrective Action (CA)Preventive Action (PA)

Example"Revise SOP-0041 to clarify the blend time specification for wet granulation batches using high-shear mixer HM-02""Review all blend time specifications across all wet granulation SOPs in the facility; update any that lack explicit time limits; retrain all production operators"
ScopeTypically narrow — addresses the specific deviation or failureTypically broad — addresses systemic risk across the facility
When it's enough aloneOnly for truly isolated, one-time events with clear assignable causes that cannot recur (e.g. equipment since decommissioned)Preventive action is most valuable but requires a systemic root cause to be meaningful

Change control is the formal process for evaluating, approving, implementing, and verifying any change to a validated or controlled GMP system. In pharma manufacturing, nothing is changed informally. Even a change that seems trivial — a new brand of cleaning wipes, a different printer for labels — must go through change control because its impact on product quality may not be obvious without a systematic assessment.

Validation is the documented evidence that a process, equipment, analytical method, or cleaning procedure consistently performs as intended and produces results meeting predetermined acceptance criteria. Before manufacturing a product for patient use, the process must be validated. Before using a test method to make a release decision, it must be validated. Validation is the foundation that GMP compliance rests on.

Validation types and what they prove
Validation typeWhat it provesWhen required
IQ — Installation QualificationThe equipment or system was installed correctly according to the manufacturer's specifications and design intent. Correct connections, correct utilities, correct software version, documentation receivedEvery new piece of equipment, system, or facility installation before use in GMP operations
OQ — Operational QualificationThe equipment or system operates correctly across its full operational range — not just at one set point. Demonstrates that when you set a temperature to 40°C, it reaches and holds 40°C; when you set a speed to 100 RPM, it reaches and holds 100 RPM. Challenges upper and lower limitsAfter IQ; before process validation
PQ — Performance QualificationThe equipment performs correctly when running actual product under production conditions. Process parameters produce product meeting acceptance criteria consistently across multiple runs. This is where equipment qualification and process validation overlapAfter OQ; using actual (or representative) product and materials

FDA's 2011 guidance requires three stages: process design, process qualification, and continued process verification. CV — Cleaning ValidationThe cleaning procedure removes product residues and cleaning agents to below defined acceptance limits — preventing carryover to the next product or batch. Critical for shared equipment between different products. When re-validation is required

Re-qualification or re-validation is triggered by: equipment repair or modification; process change within scope of change control; persistent product quality failures; facility relocation; significant changes in raw material suppliers or grades; regulatory requirement; and periodic re-assessment (typically every 3–5 years per risk-based schedule). A process that was validated 10 years ago, with multiple equipment replacements and raw material changes since, may no longer be in a validated state — even if each individual change was documented through change control. A periodic validation review confirms the validated state is maintained.

03
Investigations Deep Dive
How GMP investigations actually work

A GMP investigation is a systematic process for identifying why something went wrong, who was affected, and how to prevent it from happening again. This section covers the full investigation framework — from initial detection through root cause analysis to CAPA closure — with the specific documentation requirements at each stage. This is what regulators examine most closely during inspections.

The single most common investigation failure

Investigators reach a conclusion before completing the investigation — writing the root cause first and then gathering evidence to support it. A proper investigation starts with the observation (what happened?), expands to evidence collection (what do the records show?), generates hypotheses (what could have caused this?), tests those hypotheses against evidence, and only then reaches a root cause conclusion. A pre-determined conclusion that ignores contradictory evidence is the most serious investigation failure mode — and it is exactly what regulatory investigators are trained to detect.

01
Phase 1 — Immediate Response
  • Stop and contain — halt or quarantine affected materials
  • Notify QA and supervisor immediately
  • Preserve all evidence — do not clean equipment yet
  • Secure raw data, printouts, and all original records
  • Document timeline precisely with timestamps
  • Open Deviation Report or OOS form within required timeframe
  • Identify scope — what batches / materials / areas are potentially affected
02
Phase 2 — Impact Assessment
  • Identify all batches potentially affected by the same cause
  • Quarantine all suspect materials until assessment complete
  • Review historical data — did this happen before at a lower severity?
  • Check distributed batches — is a recall or field safety correction needed?
  • Assess patient safety risk — is there an immediate health hazard?
  • Regulatory notification assessment — does this trigger a report?
  • Document worst-case impact even if ultimately ruled out
03
Phase 3 — Root Cause Analysis
  • Gather all relevant records: BMR, logbooks, training records
  • Interview operators and supervisors present at time of event
  • Apply systematic RCA method — 5-Why, Fishbone, FTA
  • Test each hypothesis against available evidence
  • Verify the root cause: if we fix this, would the event have been prevented?
  • Document the investigational logic — not just the conclusion
  • If root cause cannot be determined, state "inconclusive" — do not fabricate
04
Phase 4 — CAPA & Closure
  • Define CA: fix the specific problem that occurred
  • Define PA: address the systemic condition that permitted it
  • Assign named owner and specific due date to every action
  • Complete batch disposition with written QA justification
  • Set effectiveness check: what data will prove the CAPA worked?
  • Verify CAPA completion with objective evidence
  • QA formal closure with signature
Root Cause Analysis Methods
The RCA toolkit

Root cause analysis is not a single technique — it is a collection of methods applied depending on the complexity and nature of the event. The chosen method must be documented in the investigation report. "We discussed it" is not a documented RCA method.

5-Why Analysis
Most Common
Ask "why?" five or more times, with each answer becoming the next question. Forces investigators past the proximate cause to the systemic root. Example: Why did the batch fail assay? → The wrong material was dispensed. Why? → Material bins were unlabelled. Why? → Labelling SOP was unclear. Why? → SOP was never reviewed by the operations team. Why? → Review process for new SOPs excluded operations department. The last answer is the root cause — not the first.
Best for: straightforward causal chains with a clear sequence of events
Fishbone / Ishikawa Diagram
A structured brainstorming tool that organises potential causes into categories — typically the 6 Ms: Man (people), Machine (equipment), Method (process), Material, Measurement (testing), and Mother Nature (environment). Each category is systematically investigated. The fishbone forces the team to consider all possible cause categories rather than focusing only on the most obvious. Output is a visual diagram that becomes part of the investigation report.
Best for: complex events with multiple potential contributing causes; team investigations
Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)
A top-down, deductive logic tree that maps all possible combinations of failures that could lead to the observed event. Uses AND/OR logic gates: an AND gate means both conditions must exist simultaneously; an OR gate means either condition is sufficient. FTA is most powerful when the investigation needs to prove that a specific cause is necessary (not just possible). Common in validation failure investigations and contamination events.
Best for: complex system failures; proving a cause is necessary; sterility failures
FMEA — Failure Mode & Effects Analysis
A prospective risk tool used reactively in investigations to understand all failure modes of a process step and which one most likely caused the observed event. For each process step, FMEA identifies: what can go wrong (failure mode), what effect it would have, and what controls exist to prevent or detect it. In an investigation context, FMEA is used to confirm whether the identified root cause is the most probable failure mode given the existing control environment.
Best for: systematic review of process steps; confirming root cause probability; risk-based investigations
Investigation red flags — what inspectors look for
01
"Operator error" as root cause. This is almost never an acceptable root cause in a GMP investigation. It explains what happened but not why it was possible. The next question must always be: why were the conditions in place that allowed the operator to make that error? The true root cause is typically the system that permitted the error — unclear SOP, inadequate training, no engineering control, high workload, poor workspace design.
02
Timeline inconsistencies. If the investigation report states the event was discovered at 14:00 but the deviation was opened at 09:00 the next day, regulators will ask why it took 19 hours. Gaps in the timeline — especially if contemporaneous documentation doesn't match — suggest either delayed reporting or retroactive record-keeping.
03
Scope too narrow. Investigating only the batch that failed without assessing whether other batches were made under the same conditions, with the same equipment, or from the same material lot. FDA specifically expects the scope to be expanded unless there is a documented reason it cannot be.
04
CAPA without measurable outcome. CAPAs defined as "retrain" or "remind staff" without specifying what training, assessed how, with what acceptance criteria, and verified when. Unmeasurable CAPAs are inherently ineffective — and regulators know it.
05
Investigation closed before CAPA completion. The investigation and the CAPA are separate records — the investigation can be closed before CAPA implementation, with the CAPA as a separate open item. But the investigation cannot be closed as "complete" if essential CAPAs are outstanding without a clear rationale and timeline.
06
Recurring events with the same root cause. If the same root cause appears in multiple investigations over 6–12 months, the CAPA from the first event clearly did not work. Recurring deviations with the same root cause are among the most serious systemic findings — they demonstrate that the quality system is not driving real improvement.

Data Integrity & ALCOA+
The foundation of every GMP record

Data integrity is the degree to which data is complete, consistent, accurate, and trustworthy throughout its lifecycle. In GMP, data integrity is not a separate compliance activity — it is the foundation on which every other GMP document rests. A batch record that fails data integrity requirements is not a record at all — it is a liability. Data integrity failures are the leading cause of FDA Warning Letters and consent decrees globally.

Why data integrity failures are the most serious GMP violation

A microbial contamination event harms a batch. A process deviation harms a product line. A data integrity failure — falsified records, deleted results, backdated entries — harms the entire regulatory system. It means regulators cannot trust any data submitted by that manufacturer, for any product, for any batch. The consequence is typically facility shutdown, import alerts, and criminal prosecution — not just for the individuals who falsified records, but for quality management who failed to detect it. Every major consent decree of the past decade has had a data integrity component.

ALCOA — the five original data integrity principles

A
Attributable
Every data entry must be traceable to the specific person who made it, and the specific time it was made. Anonymous entries, shared login credentials, and uninitialled corrections violate this principle.
L
Legible
Data must be readable permanently — not just at the moment of recording. Pencil entries, fading ink, overwritten entries, and paper damaged by moisture or solvents violate this principle.
C
Contemporaneous
Data must be recorded at the time the activity is performed — not from memory afterward. Recording a temperature 4 hours after the measurement was taken is a contemporaneity violation even if the value was correct.
O
Original
The first record of the data is the authoritative record. Transcribing data from scratch paper, personal notebooks, or temporary notes to a GMP record creates risk of transcription error and loses the original context.
A
Accurate
Data must represent what was actually observed, measured, or performed. Rounding results to make them appear more acceptable, selecting which data to record, or recording expected rather than actual values violates this principle.

ALCOA+ — five additional principles (FDA, EMA, MHRA emphasis)

C
Complete
All data generated during a test or process must be retained — including failing results, aborted runs, and unplanned repeats. Selectively keeping only passing results is the most common data integrity violation in laboratories.
C
Consistent
Data must be internally consistent — time stamps, sequences, and cross-references must agree. A batch record showing a process ended before it started, or a test result dated before the sample was taken, indicates manipulation.
E
Enduring
Records must be durable for the full retention period. Paper stored in conditions that cause degradation, digital records stored in formats that become unreadable, or systems without backup strategies violate this principle.
A
Available
Records must be retrievable throughout their retention period and producible for inspection within a reasonable timeframe (FDA expects records to be available upon request during an inspection — typically within hours).
+
Traceable
The full audit trail — who accessed data, who modified it, when, and what it said before — must be retained. Electronic systems must generate automatic, unalterable audit trails. Manual audit trails require documented correction procedures.

Data integrity red flags — patterns that indicate systemic problems
01
Testing into compliance: performing repeated analyses until a passing result is obtained, then reporting only the passing result. Electronic audit trails in modern HPLC and dissolution equipment make this detectable — regulators review audit trails, not just reported data. The "original" result is always the one that occurred first, regardless of whether subsequent results passed.
02
Shared login credentials: multiple analysts using the same username and password on laboratory instruments. Completely destroys attributability — it is impossible to determine who generated which result. Violates both ALCOA (Attributable) and 21 CFR Part 11 requirements for individual electronic signatures.
03
Backdating entries: recording events after the fact with the time and date they should have occurred rather than when the record was actually made. Even if the data itself is accurate, backdating destroys contemporaneity. When discovered — through audit trails, ink dating, or witness accounts — it creates suspicion about all data from that site.
04
Scratch paper culture: operators recording measurements on loose paper during production, then transcribing into the BMR at the end of the shift. The transcription step creates risk of error and the original scratch paper — the true contemporaneous record — is typically discarded. All data must go directly into the GMP record at the moment of measurement.

Retention Requirements
How long records must be kept

GMP records must be retained for defined minimum periods after batch release, product discontinuation, or expiry date — whichever is later. Destroying records before their retention period expires is a GMP violation. During an investigation or recall — which may occur years after manufacture — the records from the original batch must be retrievable in their original form.

Inspection Readiness
What regulators look for in each document

Regulatory inspectors — FDA, EMA/national competent authorities, MHRA, TGA — are trained to use documents to identify systemic quality problems. They do not read documents looking for administrative errors; they read them looking for evidence of what is actually happening in the facility versus what the documents claim is happening. Understanding their perspective makes inspection preparation meaningful rather than cosmetic.

The inspection readiness principle that matters most

The best inspection readiness strategy is not preparation for inspection — it is maintaining the documentation system correctly at all times. Facilities that "prepare" for inspections by retrospectively reviewing and improving records are creating data integrity risk. The question inspectors ask is not "is this document correct now?" but "was this document correct when it was written?" A document corrected the week before an inspection looks — and may be — falsification. Inspection readiness is not a sprint before the inspectors arrive. It is a daily operational standard.
